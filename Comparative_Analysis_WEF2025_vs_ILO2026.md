# Comparative Analysis: WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 vs. ILO Employment and Social Trends 2026

**Prepared by: Interdisciplinary Labour-Market and Future-of-Work Research Analyst**

**Purpose**: Rigorous comparative analysis identifying common insights, divergent viewpoints, unique contributions, and critical blind spots between these two landmark global labour market reports.

---

## 1. Report Overview

| Dimension | WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 | ILO Employment and Social Trends 2026 |
|-----------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Organization** | World Economic Forum (Geneva) | International Labour Organization (Geneva) |
| **Publication Year** | January 2025 | 2026 (World of Work Series) |
| **Primary Objectives** | Forecast how macrotrends transform jobs, skills, and workforce strategies over 2025-2030 | Analyse global labour market conditions, decent work deficits, and employment quality trends |
| **Intended Audience** | Employers, business leaders, policymakers, educators | Governments, trade unions, employers organizations, policymakers, development agencies |
| **Scope** | 55 economies, 22 industry clusters, 1,043 companies representing 14.1 million workers | Global coverage across all ILO member states; modelled estimates for all income groups and regions |
| **Geographical Coverage** | Economies representing 88% of global GDP; skewed toward formal-sector employers in upper-middle and high-income economies | Universal coverage including low-income countries, informal sector, and vulnerable populations |
| **Temporal Horizon** | Forward-looking: 2025-2030 projections | Retrospective and short-term: trends through 2025 with projections to 2027 |

**Key Observation**: The WEF report is fundamentally a *forecasting* instrument oriented toward business planning, while the ILO report is a *diagnostic* instrument oriented toward labour-market monitoring and policy response. The WEF title emphasizes "Future," while the ILO emphasizes "Trends" - a distinction that runs through their entire analytical architectures.

(WEF, 2025, p. 4; ILO, 2026, p. IV)

## 2. Methodological Comparison

| Dimension | WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 | ILO Employment and Social Trends 2026 |
|-----------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Primary Data Source** | Proprietary employer survey (Future of Jobs Survey 2024) | ILOSTAT modelled estimates, national statistical offices, harmonized microdata |
| **Sample** | 1,043 global employers; 14.1 million workers represented | Modelled estimates covering virtually all countries; econometric models |
| **Research Method** | Survey-based (employer perceptions/projections) supplemented by data partnerships (ADP, Coursera, Indeed, LinkedIn) | Statistical modelling, econometric decomposition, computable general equilibrium (CGE) models |
| **Nature of Evidence** | Subjective employer expectations and forward projections | Observed statistical indicators, historical time-series, modelled projections |
| **Survey vs. Statistical** | Predominantly survey-driven; perception-based | Predominantly statistics-driven; evidence-based |
| **Classificatory Framework** | O*NET cross-walked with ISCO; WEF Global Skills Taxonomy | ISCO; ISIC; ILO employment status classifications |
| **Analytical Orientation** | Employer demand-side (what firms expect to need) | Labour supply-side and structural conditions (what workers experience) |

### Strengths and Limitations

**WEF Strengths:**
- Provides unique forward-looking employer intentions data unavailable elsewhere
- Granular industry-level and economy-level projections for specific occupations
- Captures business strategy signals (reskilling plans, AI adoption intent, DEI measures)
- Data partnerships with LinkedIn, ADP, Coursera enrich skills-demand analysis

**WEF Limitations:**
- Self-reported employer expectations are inherently speculative and subject to systematic bias toward techno-optimism
- Sample is biased toward large multinational corporations; explicitly excludes small enterprises and the informal sector (WEF, 2025, p. 97)
- Employer projections have historically overestimated the pace of automation
- No worker voice in the primary dataset (ADP collaboration partially addresses this)

**ILO Strengths:**
- Empirically grounded in observed labour market data
- Universal coverage including low-income countries, informal employment, and vulnerable populations
- Rigorous econometric methodology including CGE modelling for trade uncertainty scenarios (ILO, 2026, Appendix B, p. 76)
- Tracks employment *quality*, not just quantity

**ILO Limitations:**
- Modelled estimates carry uncertainty margins, particularly for low-income countries with limited data infrastructure
- Less granular on specific occupational change and future skill demands
- Projections limited to 2027; less useful for medium-term strategic planning
- Does not survey employer intentions or business strategies

### Institutional and Ideological Orientation

The WEF operates as a multi-stakeholder platform dominated by corporate members. Its orientation is fundamentally market-liberal, pro-technology, and employer-centric. The ILO is a tripartite UN agency mandated to promote "decent work" and "social justice" (ILO, 2026, p. 96). Its orientation is labour-rights-focused, distributive, and attentive to structural inequality. These institutional positions do not invalidate either report findings, but they shape what is measured, what is prioritized, and what is left unexamined.

## 3. Comparative Thematic Analysis

### 3.1 AI and Automation

| Dimension | WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Framing** | Transformative opportunity requiring workforce adaptation | Source of uncertainty with unproven aggregate effects |
| **Tone** | Cautiously optimistic; net-positive employment outlook | Cautiously concerned; "benefits have yet to materialize" |
| **Key Finding** | 86% of employers expect AI to transform their business; AI creates 11M jobs while displacing 9M (net +2M) (WEF, 2025, p. 25) | "Latest wave of generative AI tools has not yet fundamentally reshaped employment patterns" (ILO, 2026, p. 6) |
| **Augmentation vs. Automation** | Distinguishes between automation (replacing human tasks) and augmentation (enhancing human capabilities); sector-specific ratios mapped (WEF, 2025, pp. 27-28) | Adopts Gmyrek et al. (2025) taxonomy: high exposure (automation risk) vs. medium exposure (augmentation potential) (ILO, 2026, p. 13) |
| **Youth Impact** | GenAI newly threatens knowledge-work roles (Graphic Designers, Legal Secretaries entering declining jobs for first time) (WEF, 2025, p. 26) | "Preliminary evidence suggests entry-level positions may be disproportionately affected"; youth with advanced education face greater AI exposure risk (ILO, 2026, pp. 12-13) |

**Critical Divergence**: The WEF adopts an *employer expectations* framework where AI is predominantly a business transformation driver generating net-positive job creation. The ILO adopts an *evidence-of-impact* framework, noting that "the anticipated benefits from new technologies such as artificial intelligence have yet to materialize in aggregate productivity figures" (ILO, 2026, p. IV). This is a fundamental epistemological difference: the WEF measures what firms *intend to do*; the ILO measures what *has actually happened*.

### 3.2 Future Skills

| Dimension | WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Top Growing Skills** | AI and big data, networks and cybersecurity, technology literacy, creative thinking, resilience/flexibility/agility (WEF, 2025, p. 6) | Not specifically forecasted; analysis focuses on exposure risk by education level |
| **Skills Instability** | 39% of existing skills expected to transform/become outdated by 2030 (down from 44% in 2023) (WEF, 2025, p. 6) | Not measured in comparable terms |
| **Skill-Employment Mismatch** | Gap between growing and declining jobs defined by: resilience/flexibility/agility, resource management, quality control, programming, technological literacy (WEF, 2025, p. 6) | Higher education does not guarantee lower unemployment in low/middle-income countries; "educational mismatch" noted as structural problem (ILO, 2026, p. 10) |

**Assessment**: The WEF provides the definitive global dataset on employer-perceived future skills demand. The ILO does not attempt this kind of granular skills forecasting but offers a crucial corrective: that formal skill acquisition (even advanced degrees) does not translate into employment outcomes uniformly across income groups.

### 3.3 Employability

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| Employability framed through skills gaps, reskilling strategies, and talent availability measures (WEF, 2025, pp. 49-55) | Employability framed through structural barriers: informality, working poverty, NEET status, and lack of formal employment opportunities (ILO, 2026, pp. 13-17) |
| 63% of employers cite skills gaps as top barrier to transformation (WEF, 2025, p. 49) | 2.1 billion workers in informal employment globally; 284 million in extreme working poverty (ILO, 2026, p. IV) |
| Solution: employer-driven reskilling, skills-based hiring, DEI (WEF, 2025, pp. 45-56) | Solution: economic structural transformation, social protection, formalization policies (ILO, 2026, pp. 15-17) |

**Critical Tension**: The WEF constructs employability as primarily a *supply-side skills problem* solvable through training. The ILO constructs employability as fundamentally a *demand-side structural problem* where insufficient quality jobs exist regardless of worker skills, particularly in low-income economies.

### 3.4 Workforce Transformation

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| Net 78 million new jobs projected globally by 2030 (170M created, 92M displaced) (WEF, 2025, p. 18) | Employment growth projected at 1.0% in 2026, "slightly below the average of the preceding decade" (ILO, 2026, p. XIV) |
| Structural churn of 22% of formal jobs (WEF, 2025, p. 18) | "The process of workers moving across economic activities over time has halved globally over the last two decades" (ILO, 2026, p. XIV) |
| Optimistic: proactive transformation through business strategy | Cautious: transformation is slowing, not accelerating |

### 3.5 Youth Employment

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| Youth (Gen Z under 25) identified as third-priority DEI group (52% of employers) (WEF, 2025, p. 58) | Global youth unemployment at 12.4%; 257 million youth NEET; NEET rates rising again after 2023 low (ILO, 2026, p. 7) |
| Growing working-age populations as a macrotrend driver (WEF, 2025, p. 5) | "Low-income countries face significant constraints... large cohort of young people entering the labour market" without sufficient quality jobs (ILO, 2026, p. IV) |
| Youth as beneficiaries of reskilling programs | Youth as structurally disadvantaged group facing worsening conditions |

### 3.6 Inclusion and Inequality

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| 83% of employers have DEI measures (up from 67% in 2023); women top priority group (76%) (WEF, 2025, pp. 55-58) | Women constitute only 2/5 of global employment; 24.2 pp lower LFPR than men; gender gaps "remain widespread" (ILO, 2026, pp. 9, XIV) |
| Framed as talent availability strategy | Framed as structural injustice and rights deficit |
| Corporate voluntarism as mechanism | Policy intervention and institutional reform as mechanism |

### 3.7 Informal Employment

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| **Explicitly excluded** from scope: "the informal sector remains out of scope for this report" (WEF, 2025, p. 97) | **Central concern**: 2.1 billion workers (57.7%) in informal employment globally; rate *increasing* by 0.3 pp between 2015-2025 (ILO, 2026, p. XIV) |

**Critical Blind Spot**: The WEF explicit exclusion of informal employment is perhaps its most significant methodological limitation given that informality encompasses the *majority* of global employment. Any forecast of "the future of jobs" that excludes 2.1 billion workers cannot claim global representativeness.

### 3.8 Green Transition

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| 47% of employers expect climate mitigation to transform business; green jobs among fastest-growing (Renewable Energy Engineers, Environmental Engineers, EV Specialists) (WEF, 2025, pp. 5, 15) | Climate risk and emissions discussed as dual challenge in Asia-Pacific; renewable energy strategies noted for China, India, Indonesia but "make only fleeting references to employment and jobs" (ILO, 2026, p. 40) |
| Environmental stewardship enters top-10 fastest-growing skills for first time (WEF, 2025, p. 5) | Green transition discussed primarily through climate adaptation challenges, not job creation potential |

### 3.9 Reskilling and Upskilling

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| 85% of employers plan to prioritize upskilling (WEF, 2025, p. 45); 50% of workforce has completed training (up from 41% in 2023) (WEF, 2025, p. 6) | Not specifically measured; focus on structural economic transformation as primary driver of employment quality improvement |
| Employers identify reskilling as top AI response strategy (77%) (WEF, 2025, p. 63) | "Governments, employers and workers must act together to develop and promote strategies that reduce decent work deficits" (ILO, 2026, p. IV) |
| 55% of employers call for public funding of reskilling as top policy (WEF, 2025, p. 54) | Not specifically addressed as policy recommendation |

### 3.10 Economic Uncertainty

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| Rising cost of living ranked 2nd most transformative trend (50% of employers) (WEF, 2025, p. 5); economic slowdown expected to displace 1.6M jobs (WEF, 2025, p. 5) | Economic policy uncertainty at unprecedented levels; business confidence below historical mean in 3/4 of countries (ILO, 2026, p. 3); "significant risks relating to mounting sovereign debt, trade policy uncertainty and AI-driven disruptions" (ILO, 2026, p. XIV) |
| Framed as one of five macrotrends | Framed as existential risk to labour market stability |

### 3.11 Productivity and Job Redesign

| WEF 2025 | ILO 2026 |
|----------|----------|
| Human-machine task frontier shifting: tasks done by humans alone declining from 47% to 33% by 2030 (WEF, 2025, p. 26); augmentation vs. automation distinguished by sector | Global labour productivity growth at 2.0% in 2026; "weak productivity growth in low-income countries is preventing income convergence" (ILO, 2026, p. 1); "anticipated benefits from new technologies have yet to materialize in aggregate productivity figures" (ILO, 2026, p. IV) |
| Focus on task-level redesign within firms | Focus on economy-wide structural transformation |

## 4. Institutional Perspective Comparison

### WEF: Employer/Business-Centric Worldview

The WEF constructs the future of work as fundamentally a **business transformation challenge** requiring employer-led adaptation. Its analytical framework centres on:

- **Firms as primary agents of change**: Survey respondents are C-suite executives projecting what *their organizations* will do
- **Skills gaps as the core problem**: 63% of employers identify skills gaps as the primary barrier (WEF, 2025, p. 49)
- **Market-based solutions**: Skills-based hiring, corporate reskilling, talent mobility, DEI as talent strategy
- **Technology as net-positive**: Job creation exceeds displacement; augmentation preferred framing
- **Workers as inputs**: The language is of "talent availability," "workforce strategies," and "human capital"

**Priorities shaped by this worldview:**
- Reskilling and upskilling (employer-driven)
- Policy flexibility (hiring/firing practices ranked 4th among desired policies) (WEF, 2025, p. 54)
- Business-friendly regulation
- Technology adoption acceleration

### ILO: Labour/Public-Policy Worldview

The ILO constructs the future of work as fundamentally a **social justice challenge** requiring institutional intervention. Its analytical framework centres on:

- **Workers as rights-holders**: Focus on decent work, social protection, labour standards
- **Structural inequality as the core problem**: Informality, working poverty, gender gaps, income-group disparities
- **Institutional solutions**: Collective bargaining, social protection, public investment, labour regulation
- **Technology as uncertain**: Benefits unproven; risks to vulnerable populations emphasized
- **Economy-wide structural transformation**: Movement from agriculture to manufacturing to services as primary driver of employment quality

**Priorities shaped by this worldview:**
- Reducing decent work deficits
- Formalizing informal employment
- Addressing gender gaps institutionally
- Trade policy that delivers inclusive outcomes
- Public debt management to preserve fiscal space for social investment

### How Institutional Differences Shape Interpretation

| Area | WEF Interpretation | ILO Interpretation |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Low unemployment (4.9%) | Positive sign; labour market health enabling transformation | Masks massive underemployment; jobs gap of 408 million (ILO, 2026, p. XIV) |
| AI adoption | Opportunity requiring business readiness | Risk requiring monitoring, especially for youth and entry-level workers |
| Reskilling | Corporate responsibility and competitive necessity | Public good requiring government investment and worker protection |
| Job displacement | Manageable through transition strategies | Potentially devastating without social protection nets |
| Informal work | Out of scope | The defining labour market challenge of our era |

## 5. India-Specific Comparative Insights

### WEF on India (WEF, 2025, p. 68)

- **Macro drivers**: "Increased digital access, geopolitical tensions and climate-mitigation efforts are the primary trends expected to shape the future of jobs in India by 2030"
- **Technology adoption**: Companies operating in India investing in AI, robotics, energy technologies; 35% expect semiconductor/computing technologies to transform operations (above global average of 20%); 21% expect quantum/encryption (above global 12%)
- **Fastest-growing roles**: Big Data Specialists, AI and Machine Learning Specialists, Security Management Specialists
- **Talent strategies**: Tapping diverse talent pools (67% vs. 47% globally); skills-based hiring removing degree requirements (30% vs. 19% globally)

### ILO on India (ILO, 2026, pp. 36-40, 94)

- **Growth outlook**: "India is expected to exhibit among the highest growth in the region, keeping the GDP growth rate in Southern Asia elevated" (ILO, 2026, p. 36)
- **Manufacturing**: India accounts for 3% of world manufacturing in current US dollar terms (compared to China at 27%) (ILO, 2026, p. 39)
- **Green transition**: India cited alongside China and Indonesia for "large-scale renewable energy strategies" but noted that these "make only fleeting references to employment and jobs" (ILO, 2026, p. 40)
- **Income classification**: India classified as *lower-middle-income* country (ILO, 2026, p. 94)
- **Digital services**: India cited as key node in digitally delivered services value chains ("software design in India") (ILO, 2026, p. 61)

### Comparative India-Specific Synthesis

| Dimension | WEF Lens | ILO Lens | Gap/Tension |
|-----------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Growth narrative** | India as tech-transformation leader | India as high-growth lower-middle-income economy | WEF sees India corporates; ILO sees India structural challenges |
| **Employment challenge** | Skills gaps; need for AI-ready workforce | 83%+ informality in income group; decent work deficits | Radically different conceptions of "India labour market" |
| **Youth** | Fast-growing tech roles for young workforce | Large youth cohort without sufficient quality jobs | WEF sees opportunity; ILO sees risk |
| **Education-to-work** | Skills-based hiring can expand talent pool | Higher education does not guarantee lower unemployment in lower-middle-income countries (ILO, 2026, p. 10) | Both report disconnect but for different reasons |
| **Informality** | Not addressed for India | India exists in income group where informality is dominant (~83%) | Vast majority of India workforce invisible to WEF analysis |
| **Green transition** | Green/energy roles growing in India | India green strategies inadequately address employment | Policy implementation gap identified |
| **Demographics** | Growing working-age population as opportunity | Demographic dividend at risk without productivity growth and quality jobs | Same fact; opposite implications |

### Implications for India

1. **India dual economy is invisible in WEF data**: The WEF captures India formal, corporate, tech-oriented sector. India informal sector employs the overwhelming majority. The ILO classification of India as lower-middle-income with 83%+ informality rates reveals WEF India insights apply to perhaps 15-20% of the actual workforce.

2. **Skills development paradox**: WEF identifies employer demand for AI, big data, cybersecurity skills in India. ILO identifies that advanced degrees do not reduce unemployment in lower-middle-income countries due to structural mismatches. Both are correct: India simultaneously has skills shortage in formal tech sector and degree-employment mismatch in broader economy.

3. **Demographic dividend conditionality**: WEF treats India growing working-age population as business opportunity. ILO explicitly warns that without "sufficient productivity growth," demographic dividends "fail to materialize" (ILO, 2026, p. 9). India position is precarious: the dividend window requires job quality improvements that are currently stalling globally.

## 6. Convergence, Divergence, and Blind Spot Analysis

### A. Common Insights

Both reports converge on:

1. **Global unemployment at historic lows (4.9%)**: Both cite this figure, though they interpret it differently
2. **Technology (especially AI) as defining force**: Both identify AI as transformative macrotrend
3. **Demographic divergence matters**: Aging in high-income vs. growing populations in lower-income economies recognized by both
4. **Youth face distinctive challenges**: Both flag youth employment as problematic
5. **Geoeconomic fragmentation disrupting labour markets**: Trade restrictions and geopolitical tensions recognized by both
6. **Skills gaps exist globally**: Whether as employer-reported barriers or structural mismatches
7. **Green transition creates employment opportunities and disruptions**: Both acknowledge

### B. Divergent Perspectives

| Dimension | WEF Position | ILO Position |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Net employment outlook** | Strongly net-positive: +78M jobs by 2030 | Tepid: 1.0% annual growth; declining in high-income countries |
| **AI interpretation** | Net job creator (+2M); augmentation opportunity | "Benefits have yet to materialize"; threat to entry-level positions |
| **Pace of transformation** | Accelerating (22% structural churn) | Slowing: structural change has halved |
| **Skills instability** | Declining (39% from 44%); reskilling working | Not measured; structural mismatch persistent |
| **Optimism/Pessimism** | "Strongly net-positive global employment outlook" | "Little progress in reducing decent work deficits" |
| **Primary risk** | Skills gaps preventing business transformation | Decent work deficits, informality, working poverty |
| **Solution architecture** | Employer-led adaptation with policy support | Tripartite action; institutional reform |
| **Labour market model** | Dynamic, flexible, skills-driven | Rights-based, protection-oriented, institutionally mediated |

### C. Unique Contributions

**WEF contributes uniquely:**
- Granular occupational forecasts (specific fastest-growing/declining jobs with percentage estimates)
- Employer AI adoption strategies and workforce response plans
- Human-machine task frontier quantification by industry
- Skills taxonomy and skills instability measurement
- Industry-specific and economy-specific data profiles
- Data partnerships revealing real-time skills demand

**ILO contributes uniquely:**
- Informal employment measurement and trends (2.1 billion workers)
- Working poverty data (284 million in extreme poverty)
- CGE modelling of trade uncertainty impacts on labour income
- Employment linked to foreign demand quantification (465 million jobs)
- Decent work indicators across all employment dimensions
- Debt crisis-unemployment nexus analysis
- Gender analysis grounded in labour force participation rates
- Structural economic transformation decomposition

### D. Shared Blind Spots

Both reports insufficiently address:

1. **Implementation realities of reskilling at scale**: Neither rigorously examines whether large-scale reskilling programs actually work, completion rates, or political economy of funding

2. **India informal economy specifically**: WEF excludes by design; ILO provides income-group aggregates without India-specific informal employment analysis

3. **Education system structural limitations**: Both identify mismatches but neither analyses *why* education systems persistently fail

4. **Platform and gig economy workers**: Neither provides substantive analysis of the growing platform economy

5. **Mental health and social consequences of workforce transition**: Both treat transition as primarily economic phenomenon

6. **Small and medium enterprise perspective**: WEF surveys large firms; ILO provides macro statistics; neither captures SME dynamics

7. **Migration as workforce strategy**: Neither provides adequate analysis of international migration intersection with AI disruption and skills mobility

8. **Subnational disparities**: Neither addresses within-country variation systematically

9. **Political feasibility of recommendations**: Both recommend interventions without assessing political economy of implementation

## 7. Critical Evaluation

| Evaluation Criterion | Stronger Report | Reasoning |
|---------------------|----------------|-----------|
| **Forecasting future skills** | **WEF** | Unmatched granularity; specific occupations and skills tracked; data partnerships provide empirical grounding |
| **Labour-market realism** | **ILO** | Grounded in observed data; covers informal employment, working poverty, structural conditions |
| **Policy-usefulness** | **ILO** | Addresses structural policy levers; framed for government action |
| **Employer-usefulness** | **WEF** | Designed for business planning; industry profiles serve as benchmarking tools |
| **Employability strategy (governments)** | **ILO** | Identifies structural barriers that reskilling alone cannot solve |
| **Employability strategy (individuals)** | **WEF** | Identifies specific skills with rising demand; actionable for career planning |
| **Developing country relevance** | **ILO** | Covers low-income economies, informality, working poverty |
| **Methodological rigour** | **ILO** | Econometric models; less subject to perception bias |
| **Forward-looking utility** | **WEF** | Five-year horizon enables planning; captures emerging signals |

## 8. Final Integrated Synthesis

### What Both Reports Collectively Reveal

**On the future of work globally:**

The global labour market in 2025-2030 is characterized by a fundamental paradox: headline unemployment is at historic lows (4.9%), yet deep deficits in employment *quality* persist and are, in many dimensions, worsening. The WEF captures one side - within the formal economy, significant structural transformation is occurring, creating new roles, demanding new skills, and driving substantial business adaptation. The ILO captures the other - for the majority of workers (2.1 billion in informal employment), this transformation is largely irrelevant, and structural conditions required for meaningful improvement are stalling.

**On workforce transformation:**

A critical tension exists between the WEF finding that transformation is accelerating (22% churn in formal employment) and the ILO finding that it is decelerating (structural change has halved globally). These describe different phenomena: within formal firms, occupational composition shifts rapidly; across economies as a whole, worker movement from low-productivity to high-productivity sectors has slowed. The implication: workforce transformation is increasingly a phenomenon of the already-formal, already-connected economy.

**On AI-driven restructuring:**

Read together, the reports suggest AI will reshape *which* tasks are performed by humans within existing formal employment (WEF contribution) but has not yet demonstrated capacity to *improve aggregate productivity or create net new employment* economy-wide (ILO contribution). The gap between employer expectations and observed outcomes should generate intellectual humility about forecasts. The most concerning finding is AI potential to disproportionately harm young workers entering labour markets - precisely the group both reports identify as facing mounting challenges.

**On employability crises and opportunities:**

The fundamental employability challenge is not singular but dual: (a) within the formal economy, a genuine skills transition requiring rapid adaptation; and (b) across the global economy, a structural deficit of quality employment that no amount of reskilling can address without underlying economic transformation. Reports addressing only one dimension (as each tends to do) produce incomplete and potentially misleading policy guidance.

**On India future labour-market trajectory:**

India sits at the intersection of both reports most significant findings and most significant blind spots. It is simultaneously: a high-growth tech economy creating AI-specialist roles (WEF); a lower-middle-income country with overwhelming informality and inadequate quality job creation (ILO); experiencing rapid demographic expansion requiring 8-10 million new quality jobs annually; and positioned in digitally-delivered services trade that could provide pathway to formalization - but also faces AI disruption risk. Neither report alone provides adequate framework for India employability strategy. A synthesis requires: WEF granularity on formal-sector skill demands + ILO realism about structural constraints + independent analysis of the informal-to-formal transition pathway that neither report adequately addresses.
