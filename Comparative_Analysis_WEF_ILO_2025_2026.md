### Primary Objectives (WEF, 2025, p. 4 — Preface)
The WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 aims to:
- Track evolving technological, societal, and economic trends to understand occupational disruption over the 2025–2030 horizon.
- Identify opportunities for workers to transition into "jobs of the future."
- Provide employers, policymakers, and educators with actionable data on macrotrends, job growth/decline, skills transformations, and workforce strategies.
- Enable benchmarking by companies and governments against industry and regional peers.

The report is organized around five macrotrends it identifies as structurally reshaping global labour markets: **technological change, green transition, geoeconomic fragmentation, economic uncertainty, and demographic shifts** (WEF, 2025, p. 9).

### Intended Audience (WEF, 2025, p. 108)
Decision-makers and experts across **academia, business, government, and civil society**; companies and policymakers benchmarking talent strategy and organizational readiness.

### Scope and Geographical Coverage (WEF, 2025, pp. 9, 97–98)
- **Forecasting horizon:** 2025–2030 (five-year outlook)
- **Economies covered:** 55 economies across 9 regions and 22 industry clusters
- **Collective GDP representation:** 88% of global GDP
- **Workers covered in dataset:** 1.18 billion (subset of ILO total employment)
- **Regions:** Eastern Asia, Oceania, South-Eastern Asia, Central & Southern Asia, Middle East & Northern Africa, Sub-Saharan Africa, Europe, Latin America & Caribbean, Northern America
- **Explicit exclusions:** Small enterprises and the informal sector (WEF, 2025, p. 97)

### Primary Objectives (ILO, 2026, pp. III–IV — Foreword)
The ILO Employment and Social Trends 2026 aims to:
- Provide a comprehensive, statistically rigorous assessment of **global labour market conditions** and their trajectory through 2027.
- Diagnose the state of **"decent work"** globally — including not just employment levels but poverty, informality, wages, social protection, and gender equity.
- Analyse structural risks including trade disruption, AI adoption, sovereign debt pressures, demographic shifts, and their implications for employment quality.
- Inform government and institutional policy toward reducing **decent work deficits** and promoting social justice.

The report's central normative framework is the ILO's **Decent Work Agenda** — encapsulating productive employment, rights at work, social protection, and social dialogue. The foreword identifies AI as raising "uncertainty for enterprises and workers alike" and frames governance of AI as central to whether it causes major disruption or enables progress (ILO, 2026, p. Governments, international institutions, social partners (employers' and workers' organizations), development agencies, researchers, and policymakers — with a strong focus on **labour rights, social protection, and macroeconomic equity**.

- **Data horizon:** Historical trends from 2005; projections to 2027

# SECTION 2: METHODOLOGICAL COMPARISON



## 2.1 WEF — Methodology

### Survey Design (WEF, 2025, pp. 97–99)
The WEF Future of Jobs Survey 2024 is the primary data instrument. It comprised 38 questions organized into five thematic parts:
1. *Business Trends 2024–2030* — macrotrend impact, technology adoption, transformation barriers
2. *Occupation Trends 2024–2030* — role growth/decline expectations; macrotrend attribution
3. *Skill Trends 2024–2030* — in-demand skills, training needs, reskilling timelines
4. *Workforce Practices 2024–2030* — talent strategies, DEI, compensation
5. *People and Technology* — automation and augmentation levels, human-technology collaboration framing

**Survey platform:** Qualtrics  
**Languages:** 12 (Arabic, Bahasa Indonesia, Simplified Chinese, French, Hebrew, Japanese, Portuguese, Russian, Serbian, Spanish, Turkish, Vietnamese)  
**Field period:** May to September 2024  

### Sample (WEF, 2025, pp. 4, 9, 98)
- **1,043 unique company respondents** — described as "more than 1,000 employers"
- Collectively employing **14.1 million workers**
- **22 industry clusters** and **55 economies**
- Respondents: Chief People Officers, Chief Learning Officers, Chief Strategy Officers, Chief Executive Officers
- Focus on **large multinational and significant localized companies**
- **Out of scope:** Small enterprises and informal sector (WEF, 2025, p. 97)

### Statistical Methods (WEF, 2025, pp. 99–101)
- **Net job growth calculation:** Weighted average of growth/decline fractions reported by employers, combined with ILO occupational employment data (ILOSTAT). Simple mean calculated per role first.
- **Labour-market churn:** Sum of absolute job losses and created jobs as a fraction of initial workforce size.
- **Dataset coverage:** 1.18 billion workers (subset of ILOSTAT total employment).
- **Reweighting:** Respondents allocated workforce fractions across six job-outlook groups.
- **Data quality controls:** Capping at 99th/1st percentile; attribution pairs with fewer than 3 votes excluded.
- **Occupational classification:** O*NET (US DOL) cross-walked with ISCO (ILO); WEF Global Skills Taxonomy for skills.
- **Economy/industry assignment:** Self-reported by respondent organizations (up to 10 economies; up to 3 industries).

### Partner Data Sources (WEF, 2025, pp. 4, 9)
| Partner | Data Provided |
|---|---|
| ILO (ILOSTAT) | Employment data by occupation for absolute job figures |
| ADP Research | Employee perspective; wage premium analysis (US market) |
| Coursera | GenAI skills demand (enrolment 2022–2024) |
| Indeed / GPT-4o | GenAI skill substitution capacity (2,800+ granular skills) |
| LinkedIn | Green hiring rates; AI job/skill concentration by industry (2016–2024) |



## 2.2 ILO — Methodology

### Statistical Framework (ILO, 2026, p. XII — Acknowledgements; Appendix A–B)
The ILO Employment and Social Trends 2026 draws on:
- **ILOSTAT** — the ILO's global database of labour market indicators derived from national labour force surveys, establishment surveys, and administrative records from ILO member states.
- **ILO Modelled Estimates (November 2025)** — produced by the Data Production and Analysis Unit, led by Steven Kapsos, and the Macroeconomic Policies and Jobs Unit. These models use regression techniques to fill gaps where national data are missing or outdated.
- **ILO Harmonized Microdata collection** — used for AI exposure analysis (Figure 1.9) using the Gmyrek et al. (2025) occupational AI exposure measure (ILO, 2026, p. 12–13).
- **OECD Inter-Country Input–Output Tables (2025 version)** — for trade and employment linkage analysis; covers 50 economic activities in 80 countries (1995–2022) with extension to 2024 via ADB multi-region tables (ILO, 2026, Appendix B).
- **IMF World Economic Outlook (October 2025)** — GDP growth projections.
- **Trading Economics** — business and consumer confidence indicators; vacancy data for 19 countries.

### Nature of Evidence
The ILO methodology is fundamentally **statistical and epidemiological** — measuring actual labour market outcomes (unemployment, informality, poverty, wages) across the entire working population including informal workers, unpaid family workers, and own-account workers. Data are **aggregate and nationally representative** rather than enterprise-sampled. Projections use econometric models grounded in historical trend extrapolation.

### Key Methodological Features
- **ILO modelled estimates for youth unemployment by sex and education** (Box 1.2, ILO, 2026, pp. 11–12): regression-based imputation for countries with missing data, using total youth unemployment rate by sex and GDP per capita as predictors; based on 97 of 167 countries with data from 2023 or later.
- **Employment linked to foreign demand** (Box 3.2, ILO, 2026, pp. 57): Input-output modelling using Leontief inverse × demand vector; covers 80 countries/territories (85% of global employment).
- **AI exposure measure** (ILO, 2026, p. 12): Based on Gmyrek et al. (2025) occupational AI exposure taxonomy: "high exposure" = large task share automated (substitution risk); "medium exposure" = partial automation (augmentation).



## 2.3 Comparative Methodological Assessment

| Dimension | WEF, 2025 | ILO, 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary data type** | Perception/expectation survey (employer-reported) | Statistical estimation from national labour surveys |
| **Unit of observation** | Firm/organization | Worker/household |
| **Sample basis** | 1,043 large/multinational employers | National statistical systems of all ILO members |
| **Representativeness** | Large formal sector only (1.18B of ~3.5B total workers) | Near-universal (covers formal + informal; all income groups) |
| **Temporal orientation** | Forward-looking (5-year forecast to 2030) | Backward-tracking + short-term projection (to 2027) |
| **Quantitative base** | Employer-weighted occupational outlook | Statistically sampled workforce-wide outcomes |
| **Informal economy** | Explicitly excluded | Core analytical domain |
| **AI methodology** | Employer perception + GenAI skill substitution (Indeed/GPT-4o) | Occupational exposure taxonomy (Gmyrek et al., 2025) |
| **Validation** | Internal cross-validation; ILO data for absolute figures | Historical data comparison; regression imputation |
| **Geographic granularity** | Economy-level profiles (55 economies) | Subregional aggregates + income group breakdowns |
| **Confidence intervals** | Not published | Modelled estimates with acknowledged limitations |

### Methodological Strengths and Limitations

**WEF Strengths:**
- Provides **employer intention data** unavailable from statistical surveys — insight into future hiring, automation, and skill investment plans.
- Rich industry-level granularity across 22 clusters.
- Longitudinal comparability across 5 editions (2016–2025).
- Access to LinkedIn, Coursera, and Indeed data provides real-time digital labour market signals.

**WEF Limitations:**
- **Survivorship and size bias:** Only large companies participate; small/medium enterprises are excluded. The 1,043 respondents represent a tiny fraction of global employers.
- **Formal sector bias:** The explicit exclusion of informal economy workers renders findings inapplicable to approximately 57.7% of the global workforce (ILO, 2026, p. XIV).
- **Employer-perception limitation:** Future projections are based on what employers *expect* to happen — not what *will* happen. Intent ≠ outcome.
- **Self-selection bias:** Respondents are WEF-affiliated organizations, likely more progressive and technology-forward than the average employer.
- **Geographic self-reporting:** Economy/industry classification relies on respondent self-identification (WEF, 2025, p. 99), introducing classification inconsistency.
- As the report itself acknowledges: "The conclusions derived for this subset should not be treated as comprehensive, but rather as providing insights on selected segments of the global workforce" (WEF, 2025, p. 22).

**ILO Strengths:**
- **Universality:** Covers formal and informal workers, all income groups, all regions — including the 2+ billion informal workers WEF cannot reach.
- **Objectivity:** Statistical measurement of actual outcomes (unemployment, poverty rates, wages) rather than reported intentions.
- **Historical depth:** 20-year trend data (2005–2025) provides structural context absent from WEF's 5-year window.
- **Income-disaggregated analysis:** Distinguishes explicitly between low-income, lower-middle-income, upper-middle-income, and high-income country experiences.

**ILO Limitations:**
- **Retrospective orientation:** Primarily documents what *has happened*; projections extend only to 2027 and are extrapolations, not forward-looking modelling of technology shocks.
- **Data quality heterogeneity:** National labour force surveys vary in quality, frequency, and methodology; modelled estimates for many low-income countries carry significant uncertainty.
- **Lack of occupation-level granularity:** Does not produce specific growing/declining job role projections.
- **Limited skills analysis:** Virtually no systematic treatment of skills transformation — a core WEF strength.
- **Limited employer perspective:** The Decent Work lens captures worker experience but not employer behaviour or strategic intent.

### Institutional and Ideological Orientation
The WEF report is produced by a **business-facing multi-stakeholder forum** whose membership is predominantly large corporations and their leadership. Its framing is inherently **supply-chain of talent** thinking: employers signal demand, workers and training systems should respond. The ILO, a **tripartite UN agency** representing governments, employers, and workers equally, approaches labour markets through the lens of rights, equity, and protective institutions. These fundamentally different ideological starting points shape not just what is measured, but what is considered a problem and what counts as a solution.

# SECTION 3: COMPARATIVE THEMATIC ANALYSIS



## 3.1 AI and Automation

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 11–12, 25–29, 43–44, 62–63)

The WEF treats AI as the single most transformative force in the global labour market. **86% of employers** expect AI and information processing technologies to transform their business by 2030 — the highest of any technology or macrotrend (WEF, 2025, p. 11). Investment in AI has grown nearly **eightfold** since the launch of ChatGPT in November 2022 (WEF, 2025, p. 11, citing Stanford AI Index 2024).

The WEF's analysis of automation vs. augmentation is nuanced. Global average tasks performed mainly by humans are projected to decline from **47% in 2025 to 33% by 2030**, with **81.5% of this reduction attributed to automation** (not augmentation) (WEF, 2025, p. 27). This marks a significant shift toward machine-led task completion. However, the WEF does not frame this as primarily destructive: it projects a **net positive employment effect** of AI (+1.8 million jobs from AI & information processing technologies specifically) and argues that GenAI's potential lies in augmenting human capabilities rather than wholesale replacement (WEF, 2025, p. 43).

Regarding knowledge work displacement, the WEF notes for the first time that **Graphic Designers and Legal Secretaries** appear among fastest-declining job roles, "illustrat[ing] GenAI's increasing capacity to perform knowledge work" (WEF, 2025, p. 26). Yet 69% of 2,800+ granular skills assessed by Indeed/GPT-4o show "very low" or "low" substitution capacity by GenAI (WEF, 2025, p. 44).

On workforce AI strategy: 77% of employers plan to reskill/upskill their workforce to work alongside AI; but 41% also plan to **downsize** where AI can replicate work (WEF, 2025, p. 63) — a significant admission buried beneath the optimism of reskilling rhetoric.

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. IV, 6, 12–13, 34–36)

The ILO takes a measurably more cautious, risk-attentive position. The Director-General's Foreword states explicitly: "the anticipated benefits from new technologies such as artificial intelligence have yet to materialize in aggregate productivity figures" (ILO, 2026, p. IV). This is empirically supported by the ILO's finding that global US GDP growth in 2025 was heavily concentrated in AI investment, but it remains unclear "how far these gains can translate into broad-based improvements in living standards and employment quality" (ILO, 2026, p. 19, citing Lichtenberg 2025).

The ILO's occupational exposure framework (Gmyrek et al., 2025) finds that **high-income country workers face the greatest AI exposure** regardless of age or education, "reflecting their specific occupational structures and task profiles" (ILO, 2026, p. 13). Critically, the ILO introduces a new concern: **educated youth in entry-level jobs** are particularly vulnerable, with younger individuals (aged 15–24) with advanced education facing *greater* automation risk than less-educated counterparts in high-income countries (ILO, 2026, p. 13).

In the Arab States specifically, the ILO quantifies AI impact as: augmentation potential at 14.6% of employment vs. automation potential at only 2.2% — suggesting net positive AI employment effects for the region — but notes benefits are unequally distributed, with women having highest augmentation potential (22.7%) yet also highest automation exposure (5.3%) (ILO, 2026, p. 36).

The ILO also raises AI's double-edged nature for informality: AI and digitalization may help **formalize informal workers** through AI-based credit assessment and digital payments (ILO, 2026, p. 38), but risks include "digital exclusion, job displacement and inequitable access to AI-driven services" (ILO, 2026, p. 38).

### Comparative Assessment

| Dimension | WEF, 2025 | ILO, 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Overall AI framing | Net-positive; opportunity dominant | Risk-attentive; benefits not yet proven |
| Primary AI impact metric | Job creation/destruction estimates; skill demand forecasts | AI exposure by occupation, age, education, income group |
| Measured AI productivity benefit | Investment as proxy for adoption | No evidence yet in aggregate productivity figures |
| Knowledge work displacement | First-time flag for creative/legal roles | Educated youth entry-level vulnerability flagged |
| Automation vs. augmentation | 81.5% of human task decline = automation | High/medium exposure taxonomy; augmentation possible |
| Who bears the risk | Declining role workers; unskilled | Educated youth; high-income country workers; women |
| Policy response | Reskill for AI; hire AI-capable talent | Governance of AI; monitoring; social protection |
| AI and informal economy | Not addressed | Potential formalizing effect but exclusion risk |

**Critical Interpretation:** The WEF's optimistic net job creation figure (+1.8M from AI technologies) exists **within a formal-sector, large-employer bubble**. These numbers say nothing about what happens to the 2+ billion informal workers, subsistence farmers, or micro-enterprise employees who constitute the majority of the global workforce. The ILO's evidence that AI benefits have not yet appeared in aggregate productivity reinforces a healthy scepticism about the timing and distributive character of AI gains. Neither report provides a rigorous causal model of how AI investment translates into worker welfare outcomes across income groups — this remains a shared blind spot.



## 3.2 Future Skills

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 32–47)

The WEF provides the most comprehensive skills analysis of any major labour market report. Key findings:

- **39% of existing core skills are expected to change** by 2030 — down from 57% in 2020 and 44% in 2023, suggesting a stabilisation of skill disruption even as AI adoption accelerates (WEF, 2025, p. 32–33).
- **50% of workers** have now completed training or reskilling — up from 41% in 2023, indicating improved employer investment (WEF, 2025, p. 32).
- Top core skill in 2025: **Analytical thinking** (cited by 69% of employers as essential) (WEF, 2025, p. 35).
- **Fastest-growing skill 2025–2030:** AI and big data (net increase score: 87) followed by networks and cybersecurity (70) and technological literacy (68) (WEF, 2025, p. 37).
- **Fastest-declining skill:** Manual dexterity, endurance and precision — the first ever net-negative skill across all WEF editions (net score: −24) (WEF, 2025, p. 37–38).
- **Reading, writing and mathematics** show a slightly declining importance trend — a deeply significant finding that the report flags without extensive analysis (WEF, 2025, p. 38).
- Human-centred skills (resilience, empathy, leadership, creativity) have all gained in importance since 2023.

The WEF's 100-worker reskilling model projects: 41 workers will not need significant training; 29 will be upskilled in their current role; 19 will be upskilled and redeployed; but **11 will need training but will be unable to access it** (WEF, 2025, p. 47).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 6, 10–13, 16–17)

The ILO does not produce a comparable skills taxonomy or demand forecast. Its skills-related findings are structural rather than prospective:

- **Skills mismatches** are identified as drivers of elevated youth unemployment in lower-middle-income countries: "youth with higher education often face higher unemployment — largely due to educational mismatch" (ILO, 2026, p. 10).
- In high-income countries, the ILO documents a **re-widening of the youth unemployment gap by education level**: young people with advanced degrees had unemployment rates 4.7 percentage points *lower* than peers without advanced degrees in 2019; this narrowed, and by 2024 the gap was reversed in upper-middle-income countries — university-educated youth now face *higher* unemployment rates (ILO, 2026, p. 12).
- European labour shortages in welders, nursing professionals, cooks, and electricians alongside surpluses in other roles, reflect "changing labour demand and skill mismatches...driven by structural factors such as ageing populations, as well as the green and digital transitions" (ILO, 2026, p. 41).
- AI exposure is higher for **educated youth** in high-income countries — suggesting skills upgrades themselves become a risk factor when they lead into AI-exposed occupations (ILO, 2026, p. 13).

### Comparative Assessment

The WEF's skills analysis is unmatched in granularity and forward-looking specificity. The ILO uniquely highlights the **education-employment mismatch paradox** — that more education does not reliably reduce unemployment, and in some contexts actively increases it. This is a critical dimension absent from WEF's framework. The WEF's 11-worker inaccessibility finding is important but understated; the ILO's mismatch analysis suggests the problem may be structural rather than merely logistical.



## 3.3 Employability

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 49–63)
The WEF frames employability primarily as a **talent availability and skills matching challenge** from the employer's perspective. Key findings:
- **63% of employers** cite skills gaps in the local labour market as the primary barrier to transformation (WEF, 2025, p. 49).
- Net talent availability outlook has deteriorated: **42% of employers expect talent to become scarcer**, vs. only 29% expecting improvement — a net score of −13% (WEF, 2025, p. 50).
- **Skills-based hiring** is gaining traction but slowly: globally 19% of employers plan to remove degree requirements; 30% in India; 38% in Saudi Arabia (WEF, 2025, pp. 61, 68).
- Dominant hiring method remains evaluation of work experience (81%), followed by pre-employment tests (48%), and university degree completion (43%) — signalling that credentialism persists even as skills-based discourse grows (WEF, 2025, p. 62).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 6–7, 10–13)
The ILO frames employability as a **structural access problem** shaped by poverty, informality, gender, and education-market mismatches:
- Global jobs gap reaches **408 million in 2026** — a broader measure of labour underutilization than the unemployment rate (ILO, 2026, p. 1).
- Advanced education does **not reliably produce lower unemployment** in developing countries; in lower-middle-income countries, youth with advanced degrees face 36.6% unemployment vs. 4.4% for those with less-than-basic education (ILO, 2026, p. 11).
- Women face a **24.2 percentage-point** gap in labour force participation relative to men (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- Young women are **14.4 percentage points more likely** to be NEET than young men (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- In low-income countries, **90.2% informal employment** (2025) fundamentally constrains employability quality regardless of skills supply (ILO, 2026, Table 1.3, p. 14).

### Comparative Assessment
The WEF diagnoses an employability crisis as primarily a **skills supply failure** — fixable through reskilling, DEI, and skills-based hiring. The ILO diagnoses it as a **structural demand failure** — insufficient quality employment opportunities exist regardless of worker qualifications. Both are empirically correct for their respective domains; the tension between them is the central unresolved question of global employability strategy.

## 3.4 Workforce Transformation

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 18–31)
The WEF projects a **net positive** workforce transformation:
- **170 million jobs created, 92 million displaced = net +78 million** by 2030 (WEF, 2025, p. 18).
- Total labour market churn: **22%** of 1.18 billion formal jobs in the dataset (WEF, 2025, p. 18).
- Largest absolute job growth: **Farmworkers (+35 million)**, Delivery Drivers, Software Developers, Construction Workers — driven by the green transition, digital access, and demographic growth (WEF, 2025, p. 23).
- Technology roles grow fastest in percentage terms: Big Data Specialists, FinTech Engineers, AI/ML Specialists (WEF, 2025, p. 19).
- Fastest percentage declines: Postal Service Clerks, Bank Tellers, Data Entry Clerks, Administrative Assistants (WEF, 2025, p. 19).
- Macrotrend creating most jobs: Broadening digital access (+9.9M net); only net-negative macrotrend: Slower economic growth (−1.6M net) (WEF, 2025, p. 25).
- Robots and autonomous systems: **−4.8 million net jobs** — the single most negative technological trend (WEF, 2025, p. 25).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 8–9, 15–17)
The ILO frames workforce transformation through **structural economic change** and the Decent Work lens:
- Global employment growth projected at **1.0% in 2026** — slightly below the preceding decade average (ILO, 2026, p. 8).
- High-income countries are experiencing a **contraction of 0.1%** in employment in 2026 — a sharp reversal from the 1.1% annual average of 2010–2019, primarily due to population ageing (ILO, 2026, p. 9).
- Low-income countries project **3.1% employment growth** but face structural inability to convert this into quality employment due to weak productivity growth (ILO, 2026, p. 9).
- The critical structural concern: **economic transformation toward higher-productivity sectors has halved** in pace over the last two decades (ILO, 2026, p. XIV). "The process of workers moving across economic activities over time has halved globally over the last two decades" (ILO, 2026, p. XIV).
- **Employment in manufacturing** across Asia and the Pacific continues to rise as a share of total employment (ILO, 2026, p. 39), driven by supply-chain shifts — contrasting with global manufacturing's declining employment share in high-income countries.

### Comparative Assessment
The WEF's +78 million net jobs projection applies only to **formal, large-employer sector** employment and is a forward-looking expectation, not an econometric forecast. The ILO's evidence of structural transformation slowdown is backward-looking but empirically grounded. The two datasets are not comparable, but juxtaposed they reveal a tension: WEF describes formal sector job creation while ILO documents the structural inability of global economies to move workers into quality employment, even when jobs exist. The informal economy — home to 2.1 billion workers — is invisible in WEF's transformation narrative.



## 3.5 Youth Employment

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 8, 57)
- Global youth unemployment: **13%** (vs. 4.9% overall) (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- NEET rate (youth not in employment, education, or training): **21.7%** globally (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- NEET rates by income group: high-income 10.1%; upper-middle 17.3%; lower-middle 25.9%; **low-income 27.6%** (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- Gen Z (under 25) is **3rd priority DEI group** for employers globally: 52% plan targeted measures (WEF, 2025, p. 57).
- Morocco: 86% of employers plan to prioritize youth in DEI measures, reflecting elevated local youth unemployment (WEF, 2025, p. 69).
- Gender unemployment gap for jobs: "in low/lower-income economies, jobs gap for women surpasses men by 7.5 percentage points" (WEF, 2025, p. 8).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 6–7, 12–13)
- Global youth unemployment: **12.4% in 2025** (crept up from 12.3% in 2024) (ILO, 2026, p. XVI).
- **NEET rate: 20.0% in 2025** (up from 19.9%), representing **257 million young people** missing education, skills acquisition, and employment experience (ILO, 2026, p. XVI).
- NEET rates in low-income countries are **17 percentage points higher** than in high-income countries (ILO, 2026, p. 7).
- New and alarming finding: **AI adoption is specifically threatening entry-level high-skilled positions** where educated youth traditionally enter the labour market — "preliminary evidence for high-income countries suggests that youth with advanced education entering the labour market may face greater difficulties because of AI adoption" (ILO, 2026, pp. XVI, 12–13).
- Young women face 14.4 pp higher NEET rates than young men globally (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- In upper-middle-income countries, women with advanced degrees now face *higher* unemployment rates than men with advanced degrees — a reversal from historical patterns (ILO, 2026, p. 12).

### Comparative Assessment
Both reports agree on the severity of youth labour market distress. The ILO adds critical granularity on the education-unemployment paradox and the emerging AI threat to educated entry-level youth — a finding with profound implications for countries like India that are rapidly expanding tertiary education. Neither report adequately addresses the systemic mismatch between education content and labour market reality, particularly in developing countries.

## 3.6 Labour Market Disruption

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 8–17, 18–31)
WEF identifies five macro-drivers of labour market disruption: technological change (AI-led), green transition, geoeconomic fragmentation, economic uncertainty (rising cost of living; slower growth), and demographic shifts. The most transformative single force is **broadening digital access** (60% of employers expect transformation) and **AI technologies** (86%). Geoeconomic fragmentation — trade restrictions, geopolitical tensions — creates modest but real disruption: employers exposed to trade restrictions are 50% more likely to plan reshoring (WEF, 2025, p. 29).

Slower economic growth is the **only net-negative macrotrend**: it is the only force projected to destroy more jobs (3M) than it creates (2M) (WEF, 2025, p. 31). Economic uncertainty raises transformation barriers significantly: 46% of employers cite organizational culture resistance; 39% cite regulatory inflexibility (WEF, 2025, p. 49).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 1–5, 17–19)
The ILO frames disruption through the lens of **uncertainty, financial fragility, and institutional risk**:
- Economic policy uncertainty indices reached **twice the 2010–2019 average** in the first half of 2025 (ILO, 2026, p. 2).
- Business confidence is **below historical mean in three quarters** of monitored countries — a phenomenon "previously observed only during the global financial crisis of 2008 and the COVID-19 pandemic" (ILO, 2026, p. 3).
- **Rising sovereign debt** is an under-discussed disruption risk: global public debt expected to surpass 100% of GDP by 2029; debt crises historically raise unemployment to peak levels and cause protracted negative employment effects (ILO, 2026, pp. 4–5, Box 1.1).
- Falling official development assistance (ODA) and restricted global migration reduce medium-term income potential from remittances — critical for low-income countries where remittances reached US$685 billion in 2024 (ILO, 2026, p. 4).
- Trade policy uncertainty may **reduce real wages** for both skilled and unskilled workers across all sectors; income losses greatest in South-Eastern Asia (up to 0.45%) and Europe and Southern Asia (up to 0.3%) (ILO, 2026, p. XVI).

### Comparative Assessment
The WEF's disruption framing is technology-centric; the ILO's is macro-financial and institutionally contextualised. The WEF misses the sovereign debt risk and ODA reduction that the ILO identifies as potentially severe labour market threats. The ILO misses the employer-level behavioural response to disruption — hiring freezes, automation acceleration, workforce strategy pivots — that the WEF documents in detail.



## 3.7 Inclusion and Inequality

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 8, 55–58)
The WEF frames inclusion primarily through **Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) programmes**:
- Women are the top DEI priority group: **76% of employers globally** plan to prioritize women (WEF, 2025, p. 57).
- DEI goals/quotas adoption doubled: from ~25% (2023) to **42%** (2025) (WEF, 2025, p. 55).
- Gender unemployment gap: female rate 5.2% vs. male 4.8%; "post-pandemic recovery lag for women" (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- In lower-middle income countries, female unemployment is 5.5% — 1.1% above male equivalent (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- In low-income economies, the jobs gap for women surpasses men by **7.5 percentage points** (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- Workers from low-income backgrounds (24%) and migrants/refugees (21%) are **least prioritized** DEI groups (WEF, 2025, p. 57).
- Wage premium gap by gender (US data): men see 44% wage premium between occupational complexity levels; women only 30% — largest at career entry (men +39%; women +19%) (WEF, 2025, p. 61).
- Lower-income and conflict-affected economies face **highest skill disruption** (Egypt, Zimbabwe: 48%) vs. lowest (Netherlands: 28%; Denmark: 29%) — a structural inequality in exposure to transformation (WEF, 2025, p. 33–34).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 9–10, 14–15)
The ILO frames inequality through **structural labour market outcomes**, not corporate programmes:
- Women constitute only **two-fifths of global employment** in 2025 (ILO, 2026, p. 9).
- Women are 24.2 percentage points **less likely than men to participate in the labour force** (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- Jobs gap rate is **4.3 percentage points higher** for women than men in 2026 (ILO, 2026, p. 9).
- Gender gaps vary far more **across regions than across income groups**, highlighting "the role of social norms and stereotypes" (ILO, 2026, p. 10).
- Gender gaps have **not narrowed** in contributing family work (women: 14.4% vs. men: 6.1%) or working poverty rates (ILO, 2026, Table 1.3, p. 14).
- In low- and lower-middle-income countries, women's informality rate is approximately **4 percentage points above men's** (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- Northern Africa has the most extreme gender gap: women's participation 49.9 percentage points below men's (ILO, 2026, p. 10).

### Comparative Assessment
| Dimension | WEF, 2025 | ILO, 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Gender framework | DEI programmes; employer intentions | Structural access barriers; actual outcome gaps |
| Primary gender metric | Employer DEI priority ranking | Labour force participation gap (24.2 pp) |
| Income inequality | Skill disruption by economy income group | Informality, poverty, wage by income group |
| Social norms | Not addressed | Identified as primary driver of gender gaps |
| Actionability | Corporate-level DEI measures | Government policy and institutional change |

The WEF's DEI framing risks **aestheticizing inequality** — presenting corporate target-setting as equivalent to structural transformation. The ILO's evidence that gender gaps are driven by social norms and regional context, not income levels, points to a much deeper and harder problem than any corporate DEI programme can resolve.

## 3.8 Informal Employment

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, p. 97)
The WEF explicitly excludes informal employment from its methodology. This is not an analytical oversight but a deliberate methodological boundary: "Out of scope: small enterprises and the informal sector" (WEF, 2025, p. 97). The report contains no substantive analysis of informal employment. The implications are severe: the 1.18 billion workers in the WEF dataset represent approximately one-third of the actual global workforce; the other two-thirds — disproportionately informal, female, and located in low- and lower-middle-income countries — are invisible in WEF's analysis.

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. XIII–XIV, 13–15, 38–39)
Informality is a **central concern** of the ILO report:
- **2.1 billion workers** projected to be informally employed in 2026 — representing **57.7% of global employment** (ILO, 2026, p. 1).
- Global informality rate **increased by 0.3 percentage points** between 2015 and 2025, reversing a prior decade of decline (ILO, 2026, p. XIII).
- Low-income countries: **90.2% informal employment rate** (2025) (ILO, 2026, Table 1.3).
- Southern Asia (India's region): **85.0% informal employment rate** (2025) (ILO, 2026, Table 2.4).
- Sub-Saharan Africa: **88.3%** (ILO, 2026, Table 2.1).
- Informality is structurally associated with: "lower job quality due to limited access to social protection, rights at work, workplace safety and job security" and "reduces productivity at both the firm and economy-wide levels" (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- The rise in own-account work (often informal, out of necessity) has **returned to 2005 levels** — reversing a decade of improvement (ILO, 2026, p. 15).
- New finding: AI and digitalization may partially **formalize** informal workers through digital financial services — credit assessment, mobile payments — but this potential is conditional on equitable platform design (ILO, 2026, p. 38).

### Comparative Assessment
This is perhaps the most consequential divergence between the two reports. The WEF's analytical exclusion of informality means its entire framework of job transformation, skills demand, and workforce strategy applies to a minority of the world's workers. Any policy or strategy that relies exclusively on WEF analysis will miss the majority of the employment challenge in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and much of Latin America.



## 3.9 Green Transition

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 15–16, 25, 30–31, 36–38, 44–45)
The green transition is a significant secondary theme for the WEF:
- **47% of employers** expect carbon emission reduction efforts to transform their business; 41% expect climate adaptation efforts to do so — ranking 3rd and 6th among all macrotrends (WEF, 2025, p. 10–11).
- Net job creation from green transition: Climate adaptation = **+5 million jobs** (3rd largest net creator); Carbon emissions reduction = **+3 million** (WEF, 2025, p. 25).
- **Farmworkers (+35 million)** top the list of absolute job creation, driven primarily by green transition trends — an important but often overlooked finding (WEF, 2025, p. 22–23).
- Among fastest-growing roles: **Renewable Energy Engineers, Environmental Engineers, Electric Vehicle Specialists** (WEF, 2025, p. 19–20).
- Green skills gap: global green skills supply grew 12% (2022–2023) but green job postings requiring green skills grew 22% — demand consistently outpacing supply (WEF, 2025, p. 16).
- **Environmental stewardship** ranks among the top 10 fastest-growing skills (net score: 53) for 2025–2030 (WEF, 2025, p. 37).
- Mining & Metals: 50% of employers cite environmental stewardship as core skill (2.5× global average) (WEF, 2025, p. 36).
- Green hiring has consistently outperformed overall hiring since May 2022 (LinkedIn data, WEF, 2025, p. 30–31).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 40–41, 59)
The ILO's green transition analysis is regional in focus, concentrated in the Asia-Pacific chapter:
- Asia and the Pacific generated **approximately 60% of the world's greenhouse gas emissions** in 2023, while being one of the most climate-exposed regions (ILO, 2026, p. 40).
- **China alone accounts for 7.4 million jobs in renewable energy** in 2024 — 46% of the global total; "India, Japan and the Republic of Korea are each also making significant headway" (ILO, 2026, p. 40–41).
- LinkedIn data shows **green jobs hiring grew 30%** in Asia-Pacific between 2016 and 2021; demand for green talent is "far outstripping the supply" in Australia, India, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, and Viet Nam (ILO, 2026, p. 41, citing LinkedIn 2024).
- Green job strategies are emerging in national development plans but early strategies made "only fleeting references to employment and jobs" (ILO, 2026, p. 40).
- Climate risk-related job displacement (flooding, drought, migration) is acknowledged as a labour market impact but not quantified (ILO, 2026, pp. 40–41).

### Comparative Assessment
Both reports affirm the green transition as a net job creator and acknowledge the green skills gap. The WEF is stronger on **global quantification of green job creation** and the skills dimension. The ILO is stronger on **climate risk as a labour market disruption** (displacement, migration, agricultural output) and the justice dimension of the transition. Neither report adequately addresses the **transition costs** — displaced workers in fossil-fuel, automotive manufacturing, or extractive industries who lack pathways to green employment.

## 3.10 Reskilling and Upskilling

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 32, 47–48)
- **85% of employers** plan to upskill their workforce as their top workforce strategy (WEF, 2025, p. 52).
- Training completion has improved: **50% of workers** have completed training (up from 41% in 2023) (WEF, 2025, p. 32).
- Funding model: Predominantly **self-funded by organizations**; free-of-cost training second; government funding third (WEF, 2025, p. 48).
- Expected outcomes of reskilling investment: Enhanced productivity (77%); improved competitiveness (70%); talent retention (third) (WEF, 2025, p. 48).
- North America has the highest workforce training need: **67% of workers** will require training by 2030 (WEF, 2025, p. 47).
- Telecommunications and IT Services sectors: 63% and 62% of workforce respectively projected to need training (WEF, 2025, p. 47).
- Key barriers to AI adoption: Lack of skills to support adoption at **50%** — ranking first (WEF, 2025, p. 63).
- AI reskilling strategy most common: 77% of employers plan to reskill workforce to work alongside AI (WEF, 2025, p. 63).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 4–5, 16–17)
The ILO's treatment of reskilling is embedded within its analysis of human capital constraints and public finance limitations:
- **Rising sovereign debt** directly limits governments' fiscal capacity to invest in lifelong learning, education, and social protection (ILO, 2026, p. 5).
- Countries experiencing debt crises face "difficult trade-offs that limit their ability to pursue labour-enhancing policies such as education, lifelong learning, social protection" (ILO, 2026, p. 5).
- Falling ODA — often concentrated in education and health — reduces the external funding available for reskilling in low-income countries (ILO, 2026, p. 4).
- Skills mismatches in Europe represent "changing labour demand and skill mismatches...driven by structural factors such as ageing populations, as well as the green and digital transitions" (ILO, 2026, p. 41).
- The ILO notes Caribbean Community (CARICOM) regional labour migration policy as a mechanism to address skills gaps — indicating reskilling is not just a domestic challenge but requires regional coordination (ILO, 2026, p. 32).

### Comparative Assessment
The WEF provides an unmatched employer-facing reskilling agenda. The ILO provides a sobering fiscal reality check: the public funding that would be needed to reach the 11 workers per 100 for whom reskilling is inaccessible (WEF figure) simply may not exist in low-income and heavily indebted countries. Neither report adequately addresses **reskilling for informal workers** — the largest group most in need of skills transformation.



## 3.11 Human Capital Development

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 47–62)
The WEF treats human capital development as an **employer-led, market-driven process** with government playing a supporting role:
- Employers are the primary funders of training: "dominant training funding: self-funded by organizations" (WEF, 2025, p. 48).
- Employers want governments primarily to fund and provide reskilling (55% and 52%) and improve public education (47%) — but not to regulate wage-setting or restrict labour flexibility (WEF, 2025, p. 54–55).
- Skills-based hiring as a human capital lever: 48% use pre-employment skills tests; only 14% give credit for short courses/online certificates (WEF, 2025, p. 62).
- Well-being: 64% of employers see promise in supporting employee health and well-being — a newly emerged top priority (up from 9th place in 2023) (WEF, 2025, p. 52).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 4–5, 17–19)
The ILO treats human capital development as a **public good requiring institutional investment**:
- Labour productivity is the bridge between human capital investment and decent work outcomes: "with appropriate labour market institutions, gains in productivity generally lead to higher wages" (ILO, 2026, p. 16).
- Labour productivity growth in low-income countries (2.0% projected in 2026) equals that of high-income countries but falls far short of the 3.6% and 3.1% rates needed in lower-middle and upper-middle-income countries for convergence (ILO, 2026, p. 18–19).
- Human capital loss from prolonged unemployment is severe: debt crises are "associated with deep recessions where employment falls on average about 15 per cent" and entail "protracted negative employment effects, with the associated greater rate of human capital loss" (ILO, 2026, Box 1.1, p. 5).
- Demographic dividend cannot materialize without matching human capital investment: "weak productivity growth and a shortage of decent work opportunities risk preventing these countries from capitalizing on a potential demographic dividend" (ILO, 2026, p. 9).

### Comparative Assessment
The WEF's human capital framework is essentially **employer-as-primary-investor**, with government as an enabler. The ILO's framework positions human capital development as **a public institutional responsibility** inseparable from social protection, education systems, and macro-fiscal policy. Both are incomplete: the WEF ignores the role of public education and social protection; the ILO's recommendations on institutional investment are under-operationalized.

## 3.12 Economic Uncertainty

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 8, 13, 42, 49–50)
Economic uncertainty is framed primarily as a **business transformation barrier**:
- **Rising cost of living** is the 2nd-most transformative macrotrend: 50% of employers expect transformation from it (WEF, 2025, p. 10).
- **Slower economic growth** is 6th: 42% expect it; it is the only macrotrend that destroys more jobs than it creates (WEF, 2025, p. 10, 31).
- IMF projects 3.2% global growth in 2025, unchanged from prior year (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- WEF Chief Economists Outlook: 54% expect economic conditions to hold steady in the short term (WEF, 2025, p. 13).
- Inflation "almost twice pre-pandemic levels" in services sector (WEF, 2025, p. 13).
- Uncertainty cited as barrier by: organizational resistance (46%), outdated regulations (39%), investment capital shortage (26%) (WEF, 2025, p. 49).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 2–5, 17–19)
The ILO provides a **deeper and more alarming macroeconomic risk assessment**:
- Economic policy uncertainty indices at **twice the 2010–2019 average** as of September 2025 (ILO, 2026, p. 2).
- Business confidence below historical mean in **75% of monitored countries** (ILO, 2026, p. 3).
- Job vacancies falling but not yet causing unemployment — firms "hesitant to hire amid uncertainty" (ILO, 2026, p. 3).
- Global public debt expected to surpass **100% of global GDP by 2029** — highest since 1948 (ILO, 2026, p. 4, citing IMF).
- Sovereign debt risk: past debt defaults see unemployment peak in the year of default, then stabilize 3 years later at a **permanently higher level** than pre-crisis (ILO, 2026, Box 1.1, p. 5).
- GDP growth in 2025 in the US "driven heavily by investment in AI... however, it remains unclear how far these gains can translate into broad-based improvements in living standards" (ILO, 2026, p. 19).

### Comparative Assessment
The ILO's economic uncertainty analysis is substantially richer. The WEF notes uncertainty as a business concern but does not engage with sovereign debt, institutional fragility, or the cascading consequences of financial crisis. This is analytically expected given their respective audiences, but the divergence is significant for any policymaker seeking a complete picture.

## 3.13 Productivity and Job Redesign

### WEF Position (WEF, 2025, pp. 26–29, 48, 52)
- The human-machine task frontier is the WEF's proxy for job redesign: tasks done mainly by humans declining from 47% to 33% of total task volume by 2030 (WEF, 2025, p. 27).
- Insurance & Pensions, Telecommunications: >95% of human standalone task reduction expected from deeper automation — highest automation intensity industries (WEF, 2025, p. 27).
- Medical & Healthcare, Government & Public: nearly half of reduction from augmentation — relatively more human-collaborative transformation (WEF, 2025, p. 27).
- 77% of employers cite enhanced productivity as the primary expected outcome of training investment (WEF, 2025, p. 48).
- 73% of employers plan to accelerate automation of processes and tasks (WEF, 2025, p. 52).

### ILO Position (ILO, 2026, pp. 17–19, 37–38)
- Global labour productivity projected at **2.0% growth** in 2026 — same as 2024 and 2025, following low growth in 2022–2023 (ILO, 2026, p. 18).
- **Southern Asia**: highest regional labour productivity growth at **3.9%** in 2026 — driven partly by India (ILO, 2026, p. 18).
- Latin America and the Caribbean: only **1.0%** productivity growth — lowest globally (ILO, 2026, p. 18).
- Global labour income share: **52.6%** in 2025 — still below 2019 level of 53.0% — "real wage growth has not kept up with labour productivity growth" (ILO, 2026, p. XVI).
- Manufacturing in Asia-Pacific continues rising as a share of employment despite automation-driven decline globally — job redesign and automation appear to be geographically redistributing rather than universally replacing manufacturing work (ILO, 2026, p. 39).
- High-income country AI investment (US data centres) drove 2025 GDP growth, but its broad-based labour impact is unproven (ILO, 2026, p. 19).

### Comparative Assessment
The WEF measures productivity through the lens of task automation efficiency and employer strategy. The ILO measures it through aggregate GDP-per-worker growth and the labour income share — revealing that productivity gains are not being shared equitably with workers. Both perspectives are essential: the WEF describes *how* productivity is being reorganized; the ILO reveals *who* benefits. The gap between them — strong productivity growth and stagnant real wages — is one of the defining challenges of the current economic moment.

# SECTION 4: INSTITUTIONAL PERSPECTIVE COMPARISON



## 4.1 WEF's Employer-Centric Worldview

The World Economic Forum was founded in 1971 as a platform for the engagement of global business leaders with public concerns. Its annual Davos meetings and research publications are fundamentally shaped by the interests, concerns, and perceptions of its **corporate and governmental membership** — which skews heavily toward large multinational enterprises, technology companies, financial institutions, and the political leaders who engage with them.

### How this shapes the Future of Jobs Report 2025:

**What it sees:**
- The labour market as a **talent pipeline** — workers are human capital to be deployed, reskilled, or replaced based on employer need.
- AI and automation as primarily **strategic opportunities** — tools to be adopted for competitive advantage.
- Skills gaps as **supply-side failures** — the education and training system is not producing what employers need.
- DEI as a **corporate responsibility programme** — measurable via targets, quotas, and training investment.
- The informal economy as **out of scope** — literally excluded from methodology.

**What it emphasizes:**
- Employer workforce strategies: upskilling (85%), automation (73%), hiring for new skills (70%) (WEF, 2025, p. 52).
- Talent availability from the **employer's perspective**: net −13% talent availability outlook (WEF, 2025, p. 50).
- Public policy requests from employers: fund reskilling, provide reskilling infrastructure, improve public education — but also **flexibility on hiring/firing (44%)** and flexibility on wage-setting (38%) (WEF, 2025, p. 54–55). These are labour deregulation requests dressed as workforce development recommendations.
- Net positive labour market outcomes: +78M jobs reinforces a fundamentally optimistic narrative.

**What it systematically downplays or excludes:**
- The 2.1 billion informal workers for whom none of these strategies are relevant.
- Worker voice, collective bargaining, or union perspectives — entirely absent.
- The consequences of the 41% of employers who plan to **reduce workforce** where AI replaces work (WEF, 2025, p. 63).
- Wage stagnation and productivity-wage disconnection.
- Sovereign debt, ODA, and macro-institutional risks.
- Implementation realities in low-income countries.

### Risk of Ideological Bias in WEF Framing:
The WEF's consistent framing of workforce disruption as a **solvable employer-management challenge** normalizes a view in which workers are adaptive resources, not rights-bearing economic agents. When 41% of employers plan to downsize due to AI while 77% simultaneously claim to plan reskilling, the contradiction is documented but not interrogated. The report presents both numbers without reconciling the fundamental tension: reskilling rhetoric and downsizing reality often coexist in corporate strategy, with reskilling announced and downsizing executed.



## 4.2 ILO's Labour-Centric and Public-Policy Worldview

The International Labour Organization is a **tripartite UN agency** — uniquely composed of governments, employers' organizations, and workers' organizations in equal standing. Its mandate is the promotion of decent work and social justice globally. The ILO's Decent Work Agenda — productive work, rights, social protection, social dialogue — serves as the normative framework against which all labour market trends are evaluated.

### How this shapes Employment and Social Trends 2026:

**What it sees:**
- The labour market as a **social institution** — producing not just income but dignity, identity, rights, and social protection.
- AI as a **governance challenge** — potentially beneficial but requiring institutional management to prevent harm.
- Skills gaps as symptoms of **structural economic failure** — insufficient quality employment opportunities, not just insufficient skills supply.
- Informality as a **central diagnostic variable** — not a marginal phenomenon.
- Gender gaps as driven by **social norms and structural barriers** — not merely employer behaviour.

**What it emphasizes:**
- Working poverty, informality, jobs gaps — measuring the quality of employment, not just its quantity.
- The 408 million jobs gap — which the WEF's unemployment rate of 4.9% obscures (ILO, 2026, p. 1).
- Labour income share and real wage growth — connecting productivity to worker welfare.
- Sovereign debt, ODA, fiscal constraints — the macro context for labour market policy.
- Gender gaps as structural and socially normed — resistant to corporate-level solutions.
- Structural economic transformation slowdown — workers not moving into higher-quality sectors.

**What it systematically downplays or excludes:**
- Employer perspectives on talent strategy, skills investment, and workforce planning — essentially invisible.
- Specific skills demand forecasts — absent entirely.
- Occupation-level job creation/destruction analysis.
- The role of entrepreneurship, private sector innovation, or technology adoption as potential positive forces beyond risk management.
- Implementation pathways for transforming informal work.

### Risk of Ideological Bias in ILO Framing:
The ILO's tripartite constitution and mandate create a strong bias toward **existing employment protection** and **institutional incrementalism**. Its recommendations consistently favor institutional strengthening, social protection expansion, and labour market regulation — without adequately addressing the political economy constraints on such measures in fiscally constrained low-income countries. Its Decent Work Agenda is the right normative destination but the pathway from where most low-income countries are — 90%+ informality, minimal social protection, weak institutions — to Decent Work is underdeveloped.

## 4.3 Comparing Institutional Perspectives on Key Issues

| Issue | WEF Framing (employer-centric) | ILO Framing (labour-centric) |
|---|---|---|
| **AI** | Strategic tool; net positive; manage transition | Risk; unproven productivity gains; governance imperative |
| **Skills gaps** | Supply failure; education/training must respond | Structural mismatch; demand failure in developing countries |
| **Unemployment** | Talent availability problem; 4.9% headline rate | Insufficient; 408M jobs gap reveals true underutilization |
| **Informality** | Out of scope; irrelevant | Core crisis; 57.7% of workforce; rising trend |
| **Green transition** | Jobs opportunity; employer transformation agenda | Climate risk to labour; just transition requirements |
| **Demographics** | Talent supply management; ageing = shortage | Demographic dividend failure in low-income countries |
| **DEI** | Corporate programme; employer commitment | Structural gender barriers; social norms; real access gaps |
| **Wages** | Growing as share of revenue (52% of employers) | Labour income share below 2019 level; real wages insufficient |
| **Policy role** | Government as enabler: fund training; ease hiring | Government as primary actor: protect rights; fund protection |
| **Worker agency** | Workers as talent resources; reskill and adapt | Workers as rights-bearing agents; social dialogue essential |
| **Risk emphasis** | Skills gaps; cultural resistance; regulatory friction | Debt crises; ODA cuts; migration restrictions; informality |

### Critical Synthesis of Institutional Perspectives

Neither institution's perspective is complete or sufficient on its own. **The WEF sees the top of the labour market iceberg — the formal, large-employer, technologically advanced economy** that represents perhaps 30–35% of global employment but generates the bulk of observable productivity, innovation, and skill demand. **The ILO sees the full iceberg — including the vast underwater mass of informal work, working poverty, and structural exclusion** that sustains billions without the productivity, rights, or protection the formal economy offers.

The most realistic and actionable picture of global workforce transformation requires synthesizing both: understanding employer strategy and skill demand (WEF's domain) while grounding it in the structural realities of labour market inequality, informality, and institutional fragility (ILO's domain). The danger is that policymakers — particularly in low-income countries — use the WEF as their primary strategic framework, designing skill development and digital economy strategies optimized for formal-sector demand while the majority of their workforce remains in the informal economy, invisible to policy and vulnerable to shocks.

# SECTION 5: INDIA-SPECIFIC COMPARATIVE INSIGHTS



## 5.1 India in the WEF Report — Direct References

The WEF report provides the most extensive India-specific data of either report, with a dedicated 2-page Economy Profile (pp. 148–149) and multiple contextual references. All data below is (WEF, 2025, pp. 12, 16, 34, 68, 148–149, 286) unless otherwise noted.

### Contextual Indicators (WEF, 2025, p. 148)

## 5.4 Education-to-Work Transition: India's Core Challenge

The combined WEF and ILO data reveals India's central employability challenge is a **structural mismatch** between education outputs and labour market demand — at multiple levels simultaneously:

**Level 1 — Credential inflation in a thin formal economy:** India has 12% tertiary attainment (WEF, 2025, p. 148) feeding into a formal economy that cannot absorb graduates at scale. University-educated youth face 8% unemployment in the formal metric (WEF, 2025, p. 148) — 4× the headline rate — consistent with the ILO's global finding that advanced-educated youth face higher unemployment in lower-middle-income countries due to educational mismatch (ILO, 2026, p. 10).

**Level 2 — Quality collapse:** The ILO's evidence from Vietnam (Nguyen et al., 2025) — that expanding tertiary education outpaced formal job creation, pushing graduates into informal employment — is likely replicable in India. As the ILO notes, the returns to education are higher in the informal sector in developing countries (ILO, 2026, p. 10, footnote 7), but this means graduates are over-qualified for the jobs available.

**Level 3 — AI-vulnerable knowledge work:** The educated formal-sector workers who do obtain quality employment face growing AI exposure. India's IT and BPO sectors — historically the engines of formal skilled employment — are directly in the path of AI-augmentation and partial automation. The WEF notes Graphic Designers and Legal Secretaries entering declining lists for the first time; India's vast IT-enabled services sector (encompassing data processing, customer service, and back-office operations) is structurally exposed to the same dynamic.

**Level 4 — Skills relevance lag:** India's education system produces graduates in disciplines that do not align with emerging demand. Only 12% of India's tertiary attainment base has exposure to AI and big data skills — the fastest-growing skill category — yet 79% of employers expect this skill to increase in importance (WEF, 2025, p. 149). The lag between curriculum change and employer demand is likely measured in years.

**Level 5 — 85% informal economy:** The majority of India's workforce — engaged in agriculture, construction, domestic work, street vending, and micro-enterprise — will not be touched by any of the above dynamics in the short term. Their trajectory is driven by monsoon patterns, MGNREGA funding, credit access, and migration, not by AI skill development or DEI programmes.

# SECTION 6: CONVERGENCE, DIVERGENCE, AND BLIND SPOT ANALYSIS



## 6A: Common Insights — Themes Strongly Shared by Both Reports

### C1. Global Unemployment Stability Amid Deep Underlying Vulnerability
Both reports document that the **headline global unemployment rate of 4.9%** (projected to hold through 2026–2027) coexists with deep structural distress:
- WEF: "Global unemployment rate 4.9% (2024) — lowest since 1991" (WEF, 2025, p. 8); yet ILO jobs gap = 402 million.
- ILO: "Global unemployment rate is projected to remain at the historically low level of 4.9 per cent in 2026" (ILO, 2026, p. III); but 284 million workers live in extreme poverty and 408 million are in the jobs gap.
- **Shared insight:** The headline unemployment rate is a misleading indicator of labour market health; both reports implicitly agree that the full picture requires broader measures.

### C2. AI Is the Most Transformative Technology of the Current Period
- WEF: 86% of employers expect AI to transform their business by 2030 (WEF, 2025, p. 11).
- ILO: AI "raises uncertainty for enterprises and workers alike and, depending on how it is governed, could cause major disruptions in labour markets" (ILO, 2026, p. III).
- **Shared insight:** AI will fundamentally reshape work — the disagreement is about how, for whom, and whether governance can shape outcomes.

### C3. Youth Labour Market Distress Is Acute and Worsening
- WEF: 13% global youth unemployment; 21.7% NEET (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- ILO: 12.4% youth unemployment in 2025 (crept up); NEET 20.0% representing 257 million young people; low-income countries 17 pp higher than high-income (ILO, 2026, pp. XVI, 7).
- **Shared insight:** Youth labour market distress is a global crisis, particularly in low-income countries, and is not improving on current trajectories.

### C4. Gender Gaps in Labour Markets Persist Globally
- WEF: female unemployment 5.2% vs. male 4.8%; jobs gap for women 7.5 pp higher in low-income countries (WEF, 2025, p. 8).
- ILO: women 24.2 pp less likely to be in labour force; jobs gap 4.3 pp higher for women; gender gaps vary more by region than income (ILO, 2026, pp. 9–10, 15).
- **Shared insight:** Gender inequality in labour markets is pervasive, persistent, and multi-dimensional — not just about unemployment rates but access, poverty, and protection.

### C5. Green Transition Creates Net New Employment Opportunities
- WEF: Climate adaptation = +5M net new jobs; carbon reduction = +3M; green hiring consistently outpacing overall hiring (WEF, 2025, pp. 25, 30–31).
- ILO: China alone has 7.4M renewable energy jobs; India and others making headway; green jobs demand outstripping supply in Asia-Pacific (ILO, 2026, pp. 40–41).
- **Shared insight:** The green transition is a real employment opportunity, but the skills gap and policy-jobs integration lag present significant challenges.

### C6. Economic Uncertainty Is a Significant Near-Term Labour Market Risk
- WEF: Slower growth is the only net-negative macrotrend; organizational uncertainty barriers significant (WEF, 2025, pp. 31, 49).
- ILO: Uncertainty indices at twice historical average; business confidence at 2008/COVID lows; job vacancies falling (ILO, 2026, pp. 2–4).
- **Shared insight:** Elevated economic and geopolitical uncertainty is depressing hiring, investment, and workforce development in ways that create real medium-term employment risks.

### C7. Demographics Are Splitting the World Into Two Labour Market Types
- WEF: "By 2050: lower-income to hold 59% of global working-age population"; India + Sub-Saharan Africa = ~2/3 of new workforce entrants (WEF, 2025, p. 16).
- ILO: High-income countries facing employment contraction (−0.1% in 2026); low-income countries facing +3.1% employment growth but insufficient quality jobs (ILO, 2026, pp. 8–9).
- **Shared insight:** Demographic divergence is creating structurally different labour market challenges that require region-specific rather than universal strategies.

### C8. The Green-Digital Skills Gap Is Real and Growing
- WEF: Green skills supply +12% vs. demand +22%; AI and big data is #1 fastest-growing skill (WEF, 2025, pp. 16, 37).
- ILO: Green talent demand far outstripping supply in Asia-Pacific (ILO, 2026, p. 41); skill mismatches driving labour shortages in Europe (ILO, 2026, p. 41).
- **Shared insight:** Both the green and digital transitions face a structural skills supply gap that is widening faster than current training systems can close.



## 6B: Divergent Perspectives — Where the Reports Fundamentally Differ

### D1. Labour Market Optimism vs. Realism
**WEF:** Net +78 million jobs by 2030; reskilling is the primary solution; technological disruption is manageable through employer strategy (WEF, 2025, p. 18).
**ILO:** "Little progress made in reducing widespread decent work deficits" (ILO, 2026, p. III); structural transformation slowdown means worker mobility into quality employment is halving over time.
**Divergence:** The WEF's formal-sector job creation numbers are real but incomplete. The ILO's structural analysis reveals that the supply of quality employment is not keeping pace with labour supply globally. Both are correct in their domains; combined, they reveal a more complex truth: formal jobs grow, but the majority of new workers — particularly in India and Africa — cannot access them.

### D2. AI as Opportunity vs. AI as Governance Challenge
**WEF:** "GenAI potential lies in augmenting human skills through human-machine collaboration rather than outright replacement" (WEF, 2025, p. 43); net positive job creation from AI technologies (+1.8M net) (WEF, 2025, p. 25).
**ILO:** "The anticipated benefits from new technologies such as artificial intelligence have yet to materialize in aggregate productivity figures" (ILO, 2026, p. III); AI threatens entry-level high-skilled positions; governance is imperative.
**Divergence:** The WEF's employer-survey-based optimism about AI may reflect elite formal-sector adaptation capacity. The ILO's aggregate productivity evidence is harder to dispute: if AI were broadly benefiting workers, the global labour income share would be recovering, not remaining below its 2019 level.

### D3. The Role of Informality
**WEF:** Informality = out of scope. Implicitly assumes the formal employment economy is representative of global labour markets (WEF, 2025, p. 97).
**ILO:** Informality is rising (0.3 pp increase 2015–2025), now covers 57.7% of global employment, and is the primary barrier to decent work advancement, particularly in Southern Asia (85%) and Sub-Saharan Africa (88%) (ILO, 2026, pp. XIII–XIV).
**Divergence:** This is not merely a methodological difference — it represents a fundamental divergence about what the labour market problem *is*. For WEF, the problem is skills gaps and talent management. For ILO, the problem is structural informality and the absence of quality employment at scale.

### D4. Interpretation of Labour Productivity Growth
**WEF:** Higher automation → higher productivity → better competitiveness; 77% of employers cite productivity as primary outcome of training investment (WEF, 2025, p. 48).
**ILO:** Labour productivity growth is occurring but "global labour income share...remained below its 2019 level of 53.0 per cent, indicating that real wage growth has not kept up with labour productivity growth" (ILO, 2026, pp. XVI, 19).
**Divergence:** This is a fundamental distributional question. Productivity is growing, but the gains are not being equitably distributed to workers. The WEF's employer-centric productivity framing treats this as a success story; the ILO's worker-centric wages framing reveals it as a failure of distribution. Both are empirically correct simultaneously.

### D5. Assessment of Workforce Development Progress
**WEF:** Training completion improved from 41% to 50% of workers (2023–2025); reskilling revolution underway (WEF, 2025, p. 32).
**ILO:** Public capacity for training investment is being constrained by sovereign debt, ODA cuts, and fiscal consolidation — particularly in low-income countries where training is most needed (ILO, 2026, pp. 4–5).
**Divergence:** The WEF measures employer-funded training in the formal sector; the ILO identifies the structural fiscal constraints on public reskilling investment. They are measuring different things and drawing different — but complementary — conclusions.

### D6. Nature of Gender Inequality
**WEF:** Gender inequality is addressable through corporate DEI programmes, pay equity reviews, and quota-setting — a corporate governance issue (WEF, 2025, pp. 55–58).
**ILO:** Gender gaps in labour force participation (24.2 pp) are driven by "social norms and stereotypes" and vary far more across regions than income groups — indicating cultural and institutional drivers that corporate programmes cannot address (ILO, 2026, pp. 10, 15).
**Divergence:** Both are correct but address different layers. Corporate DEI is a real mechanism within the formal sector. The ILO's evidence that social norms — not employer decisions — are the primary driver of women's labour market exclusion globally means corporate DEI, while valuable, operates at the surface of a much deeper structural problem.

### D7. Trade and Geoeconomic Fragmentation
**WEF:** Geoeconomic fragmentation creates modest net positive job effects (+5M combined from all geoeconomic trends); employers near trade restrictions are 50% more likely to reshore (WEF, 2025, pp. 25, 29).
**ILO:** Trade policy uncertainty reduces real wages for both skilled and unskilled workers across all sectors; income losses of up to 0.45% in South-Eastern Asia; 465 million jobs globally linked to trade (ILO, 2026, pp. XVI, 56).
**Divergence:** The WEF sees fragmentation as a strategic reallocation opportunity (reshoring, nearshoring). The ILO measures it as a wage-reducing, uncertainty-amplifying shock to global supply chains that disproportionately harms workers in trade-dependent developing countries.



## 6C: Unique Contributions — Insights From Only One Report

### WEF Unique Contributions

**U1. Occupational-Level Job Creation and Destruction Forecasts**
The WEF is the only report providing specific occupation-level projections: 170M created, 92M displaced, with top 15 growing and top 15 declining roles identified (WEF, 2025, pp. 18–23). The appearance of Graphic Designers and Legal Secretaries as declining for the first time — indicating AI's expansion into creative and knowledge work — is a significant observation available nowhere else.

**U2. Granular Skills Taxonomy and Demand Forecasting**
The WEF's 26-skill framework with net increase scores, proficiency gap analysis, and growing vs. declining skills comparison is unique and highly valuable for education planners, HR professionals, and training designers (WEF, 2025, pp. 35–42).

**U3. Employer Behavioural Data on AI Strategy**
The WEF uniquely captures that 41% of employers plan to downsize due to AI while 77% plan to reskill — documenting the coexistence of transformation optimism and displacement reality within corporate strategy (WEF, 2025, p. 63).

**U4. Industry-Level Automation Intensity Analysis**
The WEF's human-machine task frontier analysis by industry — showing Insurance & Pensions and Telecommunications at >95% automation-driven task reduction vs. Healthcare at ~50% augmentation-driven — is uniquely valuable for sector-specific workforce planning (WEF, 2025, pp. 27–29).

**U5. Wage Premium and Skills Complexity Relationship**
The ADP/WEF analysis of wage premiums across occupational complexity zones — men 44% vs. women 30% — with largest gap at career entry — quantifies the gendered return to skills in a way absent from the ILO report (WEF, 2025, p. 61).

**U6. DEI Programme Implementation Tracking**
The WEF's documentation of employer DEI programme adoption, with regional breakdowns, provides corporate benchmarking data entirely absent from the ILO (WEF, 2025, pp. 55–58).

**U7. Economy-Level Skills Disruption Comparisons**
India at 38%, Egypt at 48%, Netherlands at 28% — the economy-level skill disruption mapping provides actionable comparative data for national education policy (WEF, 2025, p. 34).

### ILO Unique Contributions

**U8. Working Poverty Measurement and Trends**
284 million workers in extreme poverty; 68% of low-income country workers in extreme or moderate poverty; poverty rising in low-income countries (2015–2025) — data completely absent from WEF (ILO, 2026, pp. XIII, 13–14).

**U9. Labour Income Share and Real Wage-Productivity Gap**
The ILO's documentation that the global labour income share (52.6%) remains below 2019 levels while productivity grows is the central evidence of distributional failure — invisible in WEF's framing (ILO, 2026, p. XVI, 19).

**U10. Sovereign Debt and Labour Market Risk**
The ILO's Box 1.1 analysis of debt default episodes showing permanent unemployment elevation and human capital loss — with global public debt heading to 100% of GDP by 2029 — is a critical and unique risk analysis (ILO, 2026, pp. 4–5).

**U11. Trade-Linked Employment Analysis**
The ILO's input-output modelling of 465 million trade-linked jobs in 80 countries, showing trade employment's role in improving formal employment rates, lower poverty, and better jobs for women and youth — and the risk from trade uncertainty to these workers — is entirely absent from WEF (ILO, 2026, pp. 56–66).

**U12. ODA and Remittance Risk**
The ILO's identification of falling ODA and restricted migration as medium-term social and labour market risks — critical for low-income countries where US$685B in remittances constitute household lifelines — is absent from WEF (ILO, 2026, p. 4).

**U13. AI Threat to Educated Youth in Entry-Level Jobs**
The ILO's specific finding that educated youth (15–24) with advanced degrees in high-income countries face *greater* AI automation risk than less-educated peers — threatening the traditional education-to-formal-employment pathway — is a unique and alarming contribution (ILO, 2026, p. 13).

**U14. Informal Employment Formalisation Through AI**
The ILO's nuanced analysis of AI's potential to partially formalize informal workers through digital financial services — with conditions on equitable access — is a constructive but cautious contribution absent from WEF (ILO, 2026, p. 38).

**U15. Ageing Population Analysis for Europe and Asia**
The ILO's detailed projection of old-age ratios in Europe and Central Asia through 2050 — Southern Europe's ratio nearly doubling to 66 per 100 — and its implications for labour force participation, productivity, and pension systems provides long-horizon structural context absent from WEF (ILO, 2026, pp. 43–44).



## 6D: Shared Blind Spots — What Both Reports Fail to Adequately Address

### BS1. The Informal Economy Transition Pathway — The Great Missing Chapter
**The gap:** Neither report provides a credible, actionable framework for transitioning the world's 2.1 billion informal workers into better employment. WEF excludes them entirely; ILO documents the problem comprehensively but offers no granular transition roadmap. The ILO's mention of AI/digital formalisation potential is acknowledged but underdeveloped.

**Why it matters:** For India (74% vulnerable employment), Sub-Saharan Africa (88% informality), and Southern Asia (85% informality), the informal-to-formal transition is the central labour market challenge of the next generation. No amount of AI upskilling strategy or reskilling programme addresses the person selling vegetables in Patna or weaving textiles in rural Tamil Nadu.

### BS2. India's Labour Market Reality — The Biggest Single Gap
**The gap:** Neither report adequately grapples with India's specific demographic, structural, and institutional complexity. WEF provides rich formal-sector employer data for India (96% AI exposure, 672.8M working-age population) but its analysis is entirely inapplicable to the 74% vulnerable employment majority. ILO mentions India in only four specific contexts (GDP growth, green energy, manufacturing share, AI exposure region). No report has done a comprehensive India labour market analysis integrating formal and informal dimensions.

**Why it matters:** India will supply nearly two-thirds of new global workforce entrants (WEF, 2025, p. 16). The global future of work *is* substantially the India future of work. A country where 85% of employment is informal, 23% of youth are NEET, only 12% have tertiary education, and the formal economy is rapidly adopting AI while the informal economy remains structurally unchanged — this is not a footnote to the global story but arguably its central chapter.

### BS3. Implementation Realities and the Execution Gap
**The gap:** Both reports identify what needs to happen (reskilling, green jobs, DEI, social protection) but neither adequately engages with the institutional, financial, and political obstacles to implementation in low-income countries.

**WEF's gap:** Calls for public funding of reskilling without accounting for fiscal constraints in heavily indebted emerging economies. Assumes organizational willingness to invest in training without modelling the free-rider problem (firms that reskill workers who then leave for competitors).

**ILO's gap:** Identifies the need for stronger institutions, better social protection, and decent work promotion without operationalizing how countries with weak states, limited revenue, high informality, and significant governance challenges can achieve these goals within realistic timeframes.

### BS4. Education System Quality and Curriculum Relevance
**The gap:** Both reports note skills gaps and education-employment mismatches but neither provides a rigorous analysis of why education systems fail to produce labour market-relevant skills, or what curricular and institutional reforms are needed.

**WEF's finding:** Reading, writing, and mathematics are slightly declining as employer priorities; AI and analytical thinking are rising. But what this means for K-12 curriculum design, teacher training, and assessment systems is not addressed.

**ILO's finding:** Educational mismatch drives elevated unemployment for advanced-degree holders in developing countries. But the mechanisms — between education institutions, employers, and government policy — that maintain this mismatch are not examined.

**Combined gap:** Neither report engages with the quality of education, not just its quantity. India's 12% tertiary attainment rate conceals massive variation in credential quality, with many degrees providing neither practical skills nor signalling value in the labour market.

### BS5. Social Consequences of Workforce Displacement
**The gap:** Both reports treat workforce displacement abstractly. Neither engages seriously with the social, mental health, community, or political consequences of mass joblessness — particularly for men in declining manufacturing roles, for young people in NEET status, or for communities dependent on single industries being automated.

**WEF:** Notes that 41 per 100 workers won't need training and 41% of employers plan to reduce workforce due to AI — but provides no analysis of what happens to the people who fall through.

**ILO:** Documents rising poverty and informality but does not analyse the social disintegration, political radicalization, or mental health consequences that typically accompany structural unemployment — issues with profound implications for social stability.

### BS6. Wage Determination and Collective Bargaining
**The gap:** Neither report adequately addresses **wages as a policy lever** for improving worker welfare. WEF mentions wages primarily in terms of employer strategy; ILO documents the labour income share shortfall but does not systematically analyse the role of minimum wages, collective bargaining coverage, or wage-setting institutions.

**Why it matters:** The documented gap between productivity growth and wage growth is not a market failure that self-corrects; it reflects the erosion of worker bargaining power, declining union density, and the weakening of wage-setting institutions globally. Neither report provides a rigorous analysis of this institutional dimension.

### BS7. Mental Health and Well-being as Labour Market Issues
**The gap:** The WEF notes employer focus on health and well-being rising to a top priority (64%) (WEF, 2025, p. 52) but frames this as a talent retention measure. Neither report analyses mental health or well-being as a systemic labour market constraint — particularly relevant given evidence linking economic uncertainty, job insecurity, and AI anxiety to worker productivity and engagement.

### BS8. Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) as Employers
**The gap:** The WEF explicitly excludes small enterprises from its methodology; the ILO's macroeconomic estimates do not disaggregate by employer size. Yet in most developing countries, SMEs provide the majority of formal employment — and in the Indian context, MSMEs employ over 110 million workers. The strategies appropriate for WEF's multinational respondents are often inapplicable to SMEs with limited training budgets, HR capacity, and access to technology.

# SECTION 7: CRITICAL EVALUATION



## 7.1 Which Report Is Stronger for Forecasting Future Skills?

**Winner: WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 — by a significant margin**

The WEF's 26-skill taxonomy with net increase/decrease scores, proficiency gap analysis by occupation type, growing vs. declining job skill differentials, and economy-level comparisons is unmatched in any comparable publication. The combination of employer survey data with LinkedIn and Coursera enrolment data and Indeed's GenAI substitution analysis produces a multi-dimensional, cross-validated picture of emerging skill demand.

**Specific strengths:**
- Granular net-score ranking for all 26 skills (WEF, 2025, p. 37)
- First-ever identification of a net-declining skill (manual dexterity, net −24) (WEF, 2025, p. 37–38)
- Proficiency gap vs. importance gap quadrant analysis (WEF, 2025, p. 42)
- Economy-level and industry-level skill comparison tables (WEF, 2025, pp. 77–93)
- Longitudinal skill instability trend from 2016–2025 (WEF, 2025, p. 33)

**Limitation:** These skill forecasts are based on employer expectations, not skills labour market outcomes. They tell us what employers *want* in the formal sector of 55 economies — not what workers in informal employment actually need or what education systems can realistically deliver.

**ILO's contribution to skills:** Limited but unique — the education-employment mismatch analysis provides crucial context for interpreting WEF's skills data in developing country settings.

## 7.2 Which Report Is Stronger for Labour Market Realism?

**Winner: ILO Employment and Social Trends 2026 — by a decisive margin**

The ILO's statistically grounded, historically deep, globally comprehensive analysis of actual labour market outcomes is definitively more realistic than the WEF's employer-expectation-based projections. Several dimensions establish this:

- **Coverage:** ILO covers 100% of the global workforce including informal workers; WEF covers ~33% (1.18B of ~3.5B total workers) (WEF, 2025, p. 22; ILO, 2026, p. XIII).
- **Measurement of outcomes vs. intentions:** ILO measures what has *actually happened* to unemployment, poverty, wages, and informality; WEF measures what employers *expect* to happen.
- **Historical depth:** 20 years of trend data (2005–2025) reveals structural patterns invisible in WEF's 5-year forward window.
- **Distributional realism:** The ILO's documentation of labour income share declining, working poverty rising in low-income countries, and structural transformation slowing contradicts WEF's net-positive narrative.
- **Macro-financial realism:** The ILO's debt crisis analysis, ODA concern, and trade uncertainty wage-reduction modelling address risks WEF does not engage.

**Limitation:** The ILO's projections are extrapolations from historical trends and lack forward-looking modelling of technology shocks, employer behaviour changes, or new business model disruptions.

## 7.3 Which Report Is More Policy-Useful?

**Answer: ILO for governments in developing countries; WEF for advanced economy and corporate policymakers**

**For governments in low-income and lower-middle-income countries** (including India's policymakers dealing with informality, youth employment, gender gaps, and working poverty):
- **ILO is far more policy-relevant.** Its framework of decent work, social protection, labour rights, and structural transformation aligns with the governance mandates of developing country labour ministries. Its income-group disaggregation allows policy calibration for different country contexts. Its trade analysis provides actionable intelligence on where employment linked to foreign demand can be built.

**For policymakers in high-income OECD countries** (managing ageing populations, digital transition, skills systems, and migration):
- **Both reports are useful.** WEF's skills forecasts inform education policy; ILO's ageing population analysis and labour shortage data inform immigration and pension policy.

**For corporate policymakers and HR strategy:**
- **WEF is decisively more useful.** Its workforce strategy data, talent availability outlook, skills demand forecasts, and industry-level benchmarks are purpose-built for organizational decision-making.

**For Indian policymakers specifically:**
- Neither report is fully adequate. A synthesis is required: WEF for formal-sector skills strategy, AI readiness, and talent development policy; ILO for informality reduction, social protection design, working poverty metrics, and gender gap analysis. India's NITI Aayog, Ministry of Labour and Employment, and state skill development agencies need both.

## 7.4 Which Report Is More Employer-Useful?

**Winner: WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 — decisively**

The WEF is purpose-built for employer utility. Its outputs — skills demand rankings, growing/declining job roles, workforce strategy benchmarks, DEI programme tracking, AI readiness indicators, and talent availability outlooks — are directly actionable by HR leaders, learning & development heads, and business strategy teams.

**Most practically useful WEF outputs for employers:**
1. Top 15 fastest-growing and declining roles (WEF, 2025, pp. 19–23) — informs hiring and redeployment decisions.
2. Core skills in 2025 ranked by employer importance (WEF, 2025, p. 35) — drives training investment priorities.
3. 100-worker reskilling model (WEF, 2025, p. 47) — workforce planning tool.
4. AI workforce strategy options with adoption rates (WEF, 2025, p. 63) — benchmarks AI-related HR planning.
5. Talent availability and development outlook (WEF, 2025, p. 50) — external hiring vs. internal development decisions.
6. Public policy asks (WEF, 2025, p. 54–55) — employer advocacy framework.

The ILO has limited direct employer utility, though its trade-linked employment analysis is valuable for supply chain planners, and its working poverty and informality data are relevant for responsible business conduct and ESG reporting.

## 7.5 Which Report Is More Actionable for Employability Strategy?

**Answer: Depends on who is designing the strategy and for whom**

**For employability strategy targeting formal sector, technology-adjacent, skill-intensive professionals:**
WEF is more actionable — providing specific skill demand forecasts, job role growth projections, and reskilling pathways.

**For employability strategy targeting vulnerable workers, informal economy participants, youth in low-income contexts, or women excluded from labour markets:**
ILO is more actionable — providing the diagnostic data (working poverty, informality, gender participation barriers, educational mismatch) needed to design interventions with genuine reach.

**For India's employability ecosystem** (skill development agencies like NSDC, vocational training institutions, state ITIs, higher education reformers):
A synthesized approach is needed:
- Use WEF skills data to identify future-relevant skill areas for curriculum design.
- Use ILO structural data to understand the majority of learners — informal sector youth, first-generation learners, women with limited mobility — and design for their contexts.
- Recognize that employability is not just a skills problem but a **structural job demand problem** — skills training without job creation at scale is a social service, not an economic strategy.

# SECTION 8: FINAL INTEGRATED SYNTHESIS



## 8.1 The Future of Work Globally — An Integrated Picture

The WEF and ILO reports, read together, reveal a global labour market that is **simultaneously advancing and fracturing** — advancing at the formal, technology-integrated frontier and fracturing at the structural foundations of employment quality, access, and equity.

**What is genuinely improving:**
- Headline unemployment remains at historic lows (4.9%) across all income groups.
- Working poverty in lower-middle-income countries has declined substantially (from 38.4% in 2005 to 10.1% in 2025 for extreme poverty) (ILO, 2026, Table 1.3).
- Digital access is expanding rapidly and is the most transformative force for new job creation (WEF, 2025, p. 10–11).
- Green hiring is outpacing overall hiring; green job creation is a genuine opportunity.
- Training completion in the formal sector has improved (50% vs. 41% in 2023) (WEF, 2025, p. 32).
- Renewable energy employment is expanding rapidly, including in emerging economies.

**What is stagnating or worsening:**
- Global informality *increased* 0.3 pp between 2015–2025, reversing a decade of progress (ILO, 2026, p. XIII).
- Working poverty *increased* in low-income countries, with 68% of workers in extreme/moderate poverty (ILO, 2026, p. XIII).
- Labour income share remains *below* 2019 levels despite productivity growth — workers are not sharing gains (ILO, 2026, p. XVI).
- Youth NEET rates are *rising* (20.0% in 2025, up from 19.9%) — 257 million disengaged young people (ILO, 2026, p. XVI).
- Structural economic transformation has *halved* in pace — fewer workers moving into higher-quality employment (ILO, 2026, p. XIV).
- AI benefits have *not yet appeared* in aggregate productivity or wages (ILO, 2026, p. III).
- Sovereign debt *rising* globally, constraining fiscal space for labour market investment (ILO, 2026, p. 4).

**The central paradox:** The world is simultaneously generating formal sector job projections of +78 million by 2030 (WEF) while experiencing a worsening jobs gap of 408 million (ILO). These numbers are not contradictory — they operate in different domains. Formal sector job creation is real but insufficient for the scale of labour supply, particularly in low-income countries. The fundamental challenge is not job creation in the aggregate but job creation of **sufficient quality, at sufficient scale, accessible to workers where they actually are** — including in the informal economy, in rural areas, without advanced credentials, and in fragile institutional environments.

## 8.2 Workforce Transformation — The Two-Speed Reality

The WEF's evidence of rapid workforce transformation — 86% AI adoption, 170M new jobs, 22% churn — describes a **fast-speed lane**: the formal, large-employer, technology-intensive economy of multinational companies, IT services, financial institutions, and knowledge workers. This lane is genuinely transforming at accelerating speed.

The ILO's evidence describes a **slow-speed lane**: the informal economy, subsistence agriculture, low-productivity manufacturing, and vulnerable self-employment where 2.1 billion workers remain. This lane is not transforming — if anything, it is growing (informality increased 2015–2025) as structural economic transformation slows.

The danger for policy is treating the fast-speed lane as representative of the whole. When WEF reports that 85% of employers plan to upskill workers, this means nothing for the approximately 400 million Indian workers in informal employment with no employer. When ILO reports that informality is rising in Africa and Southern Asia, this is directly relevant to the populations WEF's framework cannot see.

For sustainable global workforce transformation, **both lanes must be engaged simultaneously**:
- The fast lane needs better governance of AI, skills-based hiring reform, more equitable wage distribution, and continuation of green job creation.
- The slow lane needs fundamental structural investment: expanding formal employment opportunities, reducing barriers to formalization, social protection extensions, universal quality education, and job-embedded learning for informal workers.

## 8.3 Employability Crises and Opportunities — The Real Stakes

The convergence of WEF and ILO evidence reveals a multi-dimensional employability crisis:

**Crisis 1: Employability of the excluded majority.** The 2.1 billion informal workers, the 257 million NEET youth, the 284 million in extreme working poverty — these populations face an employability crisis that is not fundamentally about skills but about access, opportunity, and systemic exclusion from quality employment.

**Crisis 2: Credential inflation without quality.** In developing countries, expanding tertiary education without expanding quality formal employment is producing over-educated underemployed graduates who displace less-educated workers from the limited formal jobs available. India's 8% unemployment rate for advanced-educated workers vs. 2% overall (WEF, 2025, p. 148) exemplifies this dynamic.

**Crisis 3: AI disruption of educated entry-level workers.** The ILO's finding that educated youth with advanced degrees face greater AI automation risk in high-income countries (ILO, 2026, p. 13) — combined with the WEF's documentation of Graphic Designers and Legal Secretaries entering decline lists — signals that the traditional education-to-quality-job pathway is being disrupted precisely at its entry point.

**Crisis 4: Green and digital transition without inclusive pathways.** Both transitions are creating new jobs, but supply chains for both remain concentrated in large formal enterprises with high entry requirements. Without deliberate inclusion policy, the green and digital transitions risk deepening the two-speed divide.

**Opportunity 1:** AI-enabled formalization of informal economy — the ILO's identification of AI-based digital financial services as a potential formalisation mechanism is the most underexplored opportunity in either report.

**Opportunity 2:** Green jobs as a mass employment strategy. Farmworkers (+35 million) — driven by green transition (WEF, 2025, p. 22–23) — remind us that green employment is not only high-tech. Environmental restoration, climate-smart agriculture, and green construction can generate mass employment relevant to less-educated workforces.

**Opportunity 3:** Services trade and digital delivery. The ILO documents that digitally delivered services exports have more than doubled in a decade and now represent 14.5% of global exports (ILO, 2026, p. XVII). For countries like India with strong English-language and IT capabilities, this trade channel offers significant formal employment creation potential — and is already being partially exploited.

## 8.4 AI-Driven Restructuring — The Full Picture

The AI story told by both reports, integrated, reveals a more complex and less uniformly optimistic narrative than either report alone:

**Phase 1 (Current — 2025):** AI investment is large and accelerating (8× since ChatGPT — WEF, 2025, p. 11). Adoption is high in the formal sector. But **productivity benefits have not yet appeared in aggregate data** (ILO, 2026, p. III). Job displacement in specific occupations is beginning (data entry, bank tellers, administrative assistants declining sharply — WEF, 2025, p. 19). Entry-level educated positions are beginning to feel pressure (ILO, 2026, p. 13).

**Phase 2 (2025–2028, projected):** AI augmentation becomes the dominant experience in knowledge-work occupations — 81.5% of human task reduction comes from automation, with the remainder from augmentation (WEF, 2025, p. 27). Skills requirements shift toward AI literacy, analytical thinking, and distinctly human competencies (empathy, creativity, leadership). New jobs in Big Data, AI/ML, and cybersecurity grow rapidly in percentage terms but remain numerically smaller than displaced roles.

**Phase 3 (post-2028, speculative):** The interaction of AI with the informal economy remains the most uncertain and consequential unknown. Will AI formalise or displace informal workers? The ILO suggests potential for formalisation through digital finance (ILO, 2026, p. 38); the structural evidence of increasing informality suggests the default trajectory without deliberate policy is continued exclusion.

**The governance imperative:** Both reports converge on the need for AI governance, though they frame it differently. The WEF emphasises employer-driven skill development and human-AI collaboration strategies. The ILO emphasises institutional governance to prevent major disruptions. The critical missing element in both frameworks is a **rights-based framework for AI governance** — who owns AI-generated productivity gains, how are displaced workers protected, and what obligations do firms have when AI replaces human labour.

## 8.5 India's Future Labour Market Trajectory — An Integrated Projection

India's future of work trajectory, reading both reports together, is shaped by seven structural realities:

**Reality 1 — Demographic Scale (Evidence):** India will supply a disproportionate share of global new workforce entrants through 2050 (WEF, 2025, p. 16). With 672.8 million working-age population (WEF, 2025, p. 148) and Southern Asia labour force reaching 847 million by 2026 (ILO, 2026, Table 2.4), the scale of India's labour supply challenge is unmatched globally.

**Reality 2 — Formal-Informal Divide (Evidence):** India's labour market is effectively two economies: a dynamic, AI-active, globally competitive formal sector employing ~25% of workers; and a vast informal economy of 74–85% vulnerability (WEF, 2025, p. 148; ILO, 2026, Table 2.4) where productivity, wages, and protection are minimal. Policy that addresses only one will fail to transform the other.

**Reality 3 — Technology Acceleration (Evidence):** India's formal sector is adopting AI at above-global-average rates (96% AI exposure vs. 88% global — WEF, 2025, p. 148). Semiconductor and quantum technology adoption intentions are far above global averages (35% vs. 20%; 21% vs. 12% — WEF, 2025, p. 148). Corporate-sponsored GenAI training is at global top-2 levels (WEF, 2025, p. 12). This suggests a formal economy rapidly building digital comparative advantage.

**Reality 4 — Education-Employment Mismatch (Evidence):** 12% tertiary attainment (WEF, 2025, p. 148) feeding an economy that cannot absorb graduates at quality, while 23% of youth are NEET (WEF, 2025, p. 148). Advanced-educated workers face 8% unemployment — the education system is producing graduates faster than the formal economy creates appropriate positions. This is structurally identical to the ILO's global finding for lower-middle-income countries (ILO, 2026, p. 10).

**Reality 5 — Productivity Paradox (Evidence):** Southern Asia has the world's highest projected labour productivity growth at 3.9% in 2026 (ILO, 2026, p. 18), driven by India. But 85% informality (ILO, 2026, Table 2.4) and 74% vulnerable employment (WEF, 2025, p. 148) means this productivity growth is concentrated in a minority of the workforce and is not translating into broad-based income improvement for the majority.

**Reality 6 — Green Economy Opportunity (Evidence):** India is making significant headway in renewable energy jobs (ILO, 2026, p. 41); green talent demand is outstripping supply (ILO, 2026, p. 41); green transition drives global agricultural and environmental job creation (WEF, 2025, pp. 22–23). But India's climate strategy "makes only fleeting references to employment and jobs" (ILO, 2026, p. 40) — the jobs-climate nexus is underdeveloped in Indian policy.

**Reality 7 — Social Protection Deficit (Evidence):** A large share of informal workers remain outside social protection systems. With sovereign debt globally constraining fiscal space (ILO, 2026, p. 4) and domestic revenue mobilisation challenges, expanding social protection to the informal workforce — an imperative for managed workforce transition — faces acute financial constraints.

**Integrated trajectory:** India is poised for a bifurcated labour market future. A minority formal sector will undergo rapid AI-driven transformation with good growth prospects in technology, analytics, and green economy roles. The majority informal workforce will continue to grow in absolute numbers, driven by demographic pressure, without comparable structural transformation unless deliberate, large-scale institutional intervention connects the two economies. The central question is not whether India's formal economy will transform — it will — but whether that transformation will create pathways for the informal majority, or whether it will deepen the divide between India's two labour market realities.



## 8.6 Strategic Imperatives — What the Synthesis Demands

Reading both reports together generates a set of strategic imperatives that neither report alone makes visible:

### Imperative 1: End the Formal/Informal Binary in Policy
Current global policy frameworks — whether WEF's employer-upskilling agenda or ILO's decent work promotion — implicitly treat formal employment as the target state and informal employment as a temporary condition to be dissolved. The evidence suggests informality will persist at scale for decades. Policy must address the 2.1 billion as they *are* — designing reskilling, social protection, and technology access *for* the informal economy rather than merely *against* it.

### Imperative 2: Close the AI Governance Gap Before Phase 2 Accelerates
The window for governing AI's labour market impacts is closing. AI investment has grown 8× in three years; the displacement of entry-level knowledge work is beginning; the reskilling gap for informal workers is essentially unaddressed. Governance frameworks — including wage floors for AI-augmented work, mandatory portability of skills-based credentials, and corporate disclosure requirements for AI-driven workforce reductions — are needed before Phase 2 (2025–2028) consolidates new structures that are harder to reshape.

### Imperative 3: Build the Green-Jobs-Informality Nexus
The green transition offers mass employment potential in areas — agriculture, construction, environmental restoration, renewable installation — that are accessible to workers without advanced credentials. Policy explicitly connecting India's National Action Plan on Climate Change with employment generation for informal workers, and committing to green job quality standards, would simultaneously address climate, employment, and informality goals.

### Imperative 4: Fix the Education-Employment Pipeline
The documented credential-inflation paradox in India (and globally) requires dual intervention: both improving tertiary education quality and relevance, and deliberately expanding quality formal employment in sectors where educated workers can be productively deployed. Skills-based hiring (the WEF's 30% India figure — WEF, 2025, p. 68) is a valuable demand-side reform; equally necessary is reform of educational institutions to produce demonstrable skills rather than parchment.

### Imperative 5: Make Trade Policy Uncertainty Visible as a Labour Rights Issue
The ILO's finding that trade policy uncertainty reduces real wages for all workers — skilled and unskilled — through supply chain disruption frames trade policy as a labour rights issue, not merely an economic one. India's 55% of employers citing geopolitical tensions as a primary driver (WEF, 2025, p. 148) — highest among all surveyed economies — makes trade policy stability directly material to India's labour market outlook.

### Imperative 6: Centre Women's Structural Exclusion, Not Just DEI Programmes
Both reports document women's labour market exclusion. The ILO's evidence that exclusion is driven by social norms and varies by region — not income — means the path to change is institutional and cultural, not primarily corporate. Legal frameworks protecting women's labour rights, affordable childcare infrastructure, and land and property rights for women are more structurally impactful than any number of corporate DEI targets.
